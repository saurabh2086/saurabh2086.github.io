Bayesian Posterior Intervals

Understanding Non-Informative Priors and the Non-Invariance of HPD Regions

The Problem Statement

Context: This problem explores the properties of non-informative prior distributions and how intervals change when we transform variables.

(a) Suppose that the prior density for the standard deviation, \(\sigma\), is \(p(\sigma) \propto \sigma^{-1}\). Show that the implied prior density for the variance, \(\sigma^2\), is \(p(\sigma^2) \propto \sigma^{-2}\).

(b) Show that the Highest Posterior Density (HPD) interval is not invariant to transformation. Specifically, show that if \([a, b]\) is the HPD interval for \(\sigma\), then \([a^2, b^2]\) is not the HPD interval for \(\sigma^2\).

Understanding the Question

What is Part (a) asking?

It asks us to perform a Change of Variables. In probability, if you change your unit of measurement (e.g., from meters to meters squared), the shape of the probability curve changes. We need to mathematically prove how the density curve transforms from \(\sigma\) to \(\sigma^2\).

What is Part (b) asking?

It deals with the "most probable" range of values, known as the HPD Interval. The question claims that you cannot simply square the endpoints of the best \(\sigma\) interval to get the best \(\sigma^2\) interval. We need to prove this by showing a contradiction arises if we try.

Part (a): Solution

Step 1: Identify the Transformation

We define our new variable \(\theta\) as the variance:

\[ \theta = \sigma^2 \]

Conversely, we can express \(\sigma\) in terms of \(\theta\):

\[ \sigma = \sqrt{\theta} = \theta^{1/2} \]

Step 2: Calculate the Derivative (Jacobian)

The change of variables formula requires the derivative of the old variable with respect to the new variable:

\[ \frac{d\sigma}{d\theta} = \frac{d}{d\theta}(\theta^{1/2}) = \frac{1}{2}\theta^{-1/2} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{\theta}} = \frac{1}{2\sigma} \]

Step 3: Apply the Density Formula

We use the standard formula relating densities:

\[ p_{\theta}(\theta) = p_{\sigma}(\sigma) \left| \frac{d\sigma}{d\theta} \right| \]

Substitute the given prior \(p(\sigma) \propto \sigma^{-1}\) and our derivative:

\[ p(\theta) \propto \sigma^{-1} \cdot \left| \frac{1}{2\sigma} \right| \] \[ p(\theta) \propto \frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \]

Since we are dealing with proportionality, we drop the constant \(\frac{1}{2}\):

\[ p(\sigma^2) \propto \sigma^{-2} \]

Q.E.D. (Part a)

Part (b): Solution

Step 1: Define the HPD Condition

An HPD (Highest Posterior Density) interval \([a, b]\) is defined as the shortest interval containing a specific probability. A key property is that the probability density at the endpoints must be equal:

\[ p_{\sigma}(a) = p_{\sigma}(b) \]

Step 2: Test the Transformed Interval

If HPD regions were invariant, the HPD interval for the variance \(\sigma^2\) would simply be \([a^2, b^2]\). This would require the density of the variance to be equal at these new endpoints:

\[ p_{\sigma^2}(a^2) = p_{\sigma^2}(b^2) \]

Step 3: Proof by Contradiction

From Part (a), we know the relationship between the densities is:

\[ p_{\sigma^2}(\theta) \propto \frac{p_{\sigma}(\sqrt{\theta})}{\sqrt{\theta}} \]

Substituting our endpoints \(a^2\) and \(b^2\):

\[ p_{\sigma^2}(a^2) \propto \frac{p_{\sigma}(a)}{a} \quad \text{and} \quad p_{\sigma^2}(b^2) \propto \frac{p_{\sigma}(b)}{b} \]

If we assume the new interval IS an HPD interval, then \(p_{\sigma^2}(a^2) = p_{\sigma^2}(b^2)\), which implies:

\[ \frac{p_{\sigma}(a)}{a} = \frac{p_{\sigma}(b)}{b} \]

However, we started with the condition that \(p_{\sigma}(a) = p_{\sigma}(b)\). Canceling these terms leaves:

\[ \frac{1}{a} = \frac{1}{b} \implies a = b \]

Contradiction: A valid interval must have \(a < b\). Since \(a=b\) is impossible for an interval, our assumption was wrong.

Conclusion

The Highest Posterior Density (HPD) region is not invariant under non-linear transformations. While the logic of probability holds, the "shape" of the distribution stretches and compresses, changing which regions have the "highest" density.

Always recalculate HPDs after transformation.
