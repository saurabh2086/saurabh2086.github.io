Poisson-Gamma Conjugacy

A Detailed Step-by-Step Bayesian Solution

Problem: Show that if \( y_i \) are independent observations from a Poisson distribution parameterized by rate \( \theta \) and exposure \( x_i \), and we assume a \( \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta) \) prior for \( \theta \), then the posterior distribution for \( \theta \) is also a Gamma distribution. Derive the parameters for this posterior.

Step 1: Define the Likelihood

The Poisson PMF for a single observation \( y_i \) with mean \( \lambda_i = x_i \theta \) is:

\[ P(y_i|\theta, x_i) = \frac{(x_i \theta)^{y_i} e^{-x_i \theta}}{y_i!} \]

For \( n \) independent observations, the joint likelihood is the product of individual PMFs. We drop terms that do not depend on \( \theta \) to find the likelihood kernel:

\[ L(\theta) \propto \prod_{i=1}^n \theta^{y_i} e^{-x_i \theta} = \theta^{\sum_{i=1}^n y_i} e^{-\theta \sum_{i=1}^n x_i} \]

Step 2: Define the Prior

We assume a Gamma distribution as the prior for our parameter \( \theta \). Its PDF kernel is defined by shape \( \alpha \) and rate \( \beta \):

\[ P(\theta) \propto \theta^{\alpha-1} e^{-\beta \theta} \]

Step 3: Apply Bayes' Theorem

The posterior is proportional to the likelihood multiplied by the prior:

\[ P(\theta|y, x) \propto \text{Likelihood}(\theta) \times \text{Prior}(\theta) \] \[ P(\theta|y, x) \propto \left( \theta^{\sum y_i} e^{-\theta \sum x_i} \right) \times \left( \theta^{\alpha-1} e^{-\beta \theta} \right) \]

Step 4: Combine and Simplify

We group terms with the same base (\( \theta \) and \( e \)) by adding their exponents:

\[ P(\theta|y, x) \propto \theta^{(\alpha + \sum y_i) - 1} e^{-\theta (\beta + \sum x_i)} \]

Final Result

The resulting kernel matches the form of a Gamma distribution kernel. We can identify the new parameters by inspection:

\[ \theta|y, x \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha^*, \beta^*) \] \[ \text{where } \alpha^* = \alpha + \sum_{i=1}^n y_i \quad \text{and} \quad \beta^* = \beta + \sum_{i=1}^n x_i \]

Final Conclusion

This derivation demonstrates the mathematical elegance of conjugate priors. By choosing a Gamma prior for a Poisson likelihood, we ensure that the posterior remains within the Gamma family, allowing for straightforward, closed-form updates.

The logic of the update is intuitive:

  • The Shape Parameter (\(\alpha\)) is updated by the total number of events observed: \(\sum y_i\).
  • The Rate Parameter (\(\beta\)) is updated by the total amount of exposure: \(\sum x_i\).

As the total exposure increases, our uncertainty decreases and the posterior mean converges toward the observed rate in the data, illustrating the Bayesian transition from prior belief to evidence-based conclusion.
