Hierarchical Models and Multiple Comparisons

Why treating groups in isolation leads to overconfidence, and how Bayesian hierarchical models borrow strength to make fair comparisons.

1. The Background and Problem Statement

Suppose you are looking at the results of coaching programs in eight different schools (the famous 8 Schools dataset from Section 5.5). For each school $j$, you are given an estimated coaching effect $y_j$ and its associated standard error $\sigma_j$:

School ($j$) A B C D E F G H
Effect ($y_j$) 28 8 -3 7 -1 1 18 12
Std. Error ($\sigma_j$) 15 10 16 11 9 11 10 18

Look closely at School A. Its measured improvement is $+28$ points. School G is second at $+18$. School C has a negative result of $-3$.

The core question: Can we declare that School A's coaching program is definitely the best?

In traditional statistics, comparing all these schools simultaneously leads to the classical multiple comparisons problem: if you run a bunch of pairwise t-tests, you risk finding false positives by chance alone. To avoid this, you usually use conservative adjustments like Bonferroni, which essentially penalize your statistical power.

A Bayesian hierarchical model handles this naturally by assuming the schools are exchangeable. Instead of treating each school as completely independent ($\tau = \infty$, no-pooling) or completely identical ($\tau = 0$, complete pooling), we model them as draws from a shared population distribution.

2. The Hierarchical Tug-of-War

Let's build the model from first principles. We assume that the observed data $y_j$ is a noisy measurement of the true, unobserved coaching effect $\theta_j$ in school $j$:

$$y_j \mid \theta_j \sim N(\theta_j, \sigma_j^2)$$

Since the schools are exchangeable, we assume the true effects $\theta_j$ are drawn from a common normal population distribution governed by a population mean $\mu$ and between-school standard deviation $\tau$:

$$\theta_j \mid \mu, \tau \sim N(\mu, \tau^2)$$

Case 1: If we know the hyperparameters ($\mu$, $\tau$)

If someone handed you the true population mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\tau$, finding the posterior of $\theta_j$ would be a simple application of Bayes' rule for normal distributions. The posterior distribution for each group is independent:

$$\theta_j \mid \mu, \tau, y \sim N(\hat{\theta}_j, V_j)$$

where the conditional posterior mean $\hat{\theta}_j$ and variance $V_j$ are:

$$\hat{\theta}_j = E(\theta_j \mid \mu, \tau, y) = \frac{\frac{1}{\sigma_j^2} y_j + \frac{1}{\tau^2} \mu}{\frac{1}{\sigma_j^2} + \frac{1}{\tau^2}}$$ $$V_j = \text{Var}(\theta_j \mid \mu, \tau, y) = \frac{1}{\frac{1}{\sigma_j^2} + \frac{1}{\tau^2}}$$
The Intuition: Precision Weighting

This is a beautiful weighted average. The weights are the precisions (inverse variances). The local precision is $1/\sigma_j^2$ and the population precision is $1/\tau^2$. If local data is highly noisy ($\sigma_j^2$ is large), the local precision is small, and $\hat{\theta}_j$ is pulled strongly toward the population mean $\mu$ (shrinkage). If the population variance $\tau^2$ is small, the population precision is huge, forcing all school estimates to pool closely around $\mu$.

Case 2: If we don't know the population mean $\mu$

Of course, we never know $\mu$ and $\tau$. If we assign a uniform prior to $\mu$ given $\tau$ ($p(\mu \mid \tau) \propto 1$), we must propagate our uncertainty about $\mu$.

By integrating over the distribution of $\mu$ given $\tau$ and $y$, the conditional posterior mean and variance of $\theta_j$ become:

$$\hat{\theta}_j(\tau) = E(\theta_j \mid \tau, y) = \frac{\frac{1}{\sigma_j^2} y_j + \frac{1}{\tau^2} \hat{\mu}}{\frac{1}{\sigma_j^2} + \frac{1}{\tau^2}}$$ $$V_j(\tau) = \text{Var}(\theta_j \mid \tau, y) = \frac{1}{\frac{1}{\sigma_j^2} + \frac{1}{\tau^2}} + \left( \frac{\frac{1}{\tau^2}}{\frac{1}{\sigma_j^2} + \frac{1}{\tau^2}} \right)^2 V_\mu$$

where the estimated population mean $\hat{\mu}$ and its variance $V_\mu$ are:

$$\hat{\mu} = E(\mu \mid \tau, y) = \frac{\sum_{k=1}^J \frac{1}{\sigma_k^2 + \tau^2} y_k}{\sum_{k=1}^J \frac{1}{\sigma_k^2 + \tau^2}}$$ $$V_\mu = \text{Var}(\mu \mid \tau, y) = \frac{1}{\sum_{k=1}^J \frac{1}{\sigma_k^2 + \tau^2}}$$
Why did the variance increase?

The variance formula $V_j(\tau)$ has two terms. The first term $\frac{1}{1/\sigma_j^2 + 1/\tau^2}$ is the variance we would have if we knew $\mu$ exactly. The second term, $\left( \frac{1/\tau^2}{1/\sigma_j^2 + 1/\tau^2} \right)^2 V_\mu$, is the inflation factor representing our ignorance about the true population mean $\mu$. The less information we have about $\mu$ (larger $V_\mu$), or the more we rely on $\mu$ (larger weight on the population prior), the more our uncertainty about $\theta_j$ is amplified.

3. Part (a): The Fully Bayesian Hierarchical Model

In the full hierarchical model, we also do not know $\tau$. We assign a uniform prior $p(\tau) \propto 1$, compute the marginal posterior $p(\tau \mid y)$ numerically on a grid, and draw simulations. Drawing 1000 samples from the posterior distribution of $(\theta_1, \dots, \theta_8)$, we can estimate the probabilities of interest.

How the "Best" School is Computed

To find the probability that a given school $j$ has the most effective coaching program, we cannot simply compare the posterior means. We must account for the full joint uncertainty.

In each simulation draw $s \in \{1, \dots, S\}$, we obtain a vector of simulated effects $(\theta_{1}^{(s)}, \dots, \theta_{8}^{(s)})$. We check which school has the maximum value in that specific draw:

$$j^* = \arg\max_{j} \theta_{j}^{(s)}$$

The estimated posterior probability that school $j$ is the best is the proportion of simulation draws where it wins:

$$\text{Pr}(\theta_j = \max_k \theta_k \mid y) \approx \frac{1}{S} \sum_{s=1}^S \mathbb{I}\left(j = \arg\max_{k} \theta_{k}^{(s)}\right)$$

This simulation-based approach elegantly handles the multiple comparisons problem: a school with a high but highly uncertain estimate (like School A) will only "win" in some draws, while in other draws its effect will be simulated much lower, allowing other schools (like G) to win.

The Python Implementation

Below is the standard Python code using `numpy` and `scipy` to perform the grid sampling for $\tau$, sample the hyperparameters, and simulate the joint posterior of the school effects. 

import numpy as np
import scipy.stats as stats

# 1. Dataset (Eight Schools)
y = np.array([28, 8, -3, 7, -1, 1, 18, 12])
sigma = np.array([15, 10, 16, 11, 9, 11, 10, 18])
J = len(y)

# 2. Grid Sampling for tau (Uniform Prior p(tau) ∝ 1)
tau_grid = np.linspace(0.001, 40, 200)

def compute_p_tau(tau):
    # Precision weights for this tau
    prec = 1.0 / (sigma**2 + tau**2)
    V_mu = 1.0 / np.sum(prec)
    mu_hat = np.sum(y * prec) * V_mu
    
    # Log marginal posterior (Equation 5.21 in BDA3)
    log_p = 0.5 * np.log(V_mu) - 0.5 * np.sum(np.log(sigma**2 + tau**2)) \
            - 0.5 * np.sum((y - mu_hat)**2 / (sigma**2 + tau**2))
    return np.exp(log_p)

# Compute and normalize the grid densities
p_tau_grid = np.array([compute_p_tau(t) for t in tau_grid])
p_tau_grid /= np.sum(p_tau_grid)

# 3. Simulate from Posterior
n_samples = 10000
tau_draws = np.random.choice(tau_grid, size=n_samples, p=p_tau_grid)
theta_draws = np.zeros((n_samples, J))

for i in range(n_samples):
    tau = tau_draws[i]
    
    # Draw mu from p(mu | tau, y)
    prec = 1.0 / (sigma**2 + tau**2)
    V_mu = 1.0 / np.sum(prec)
    mu_hat = np.sum(y * prec) * V_mu
    mu = np.random.normal(mu_hat, np.sqrt(V_mu))
    
    # Draw theta_j from p(theta_j | mu, tau, y)
    V_j = 1.0 / (1.0 / sigma**2 + 1.0 / tau**2)
    theta_hat_j = V_j * (y / sigma**2 + mu / tau**2)
    theta_draws[i, :] = np.random.normal(theta_hat_j, np.sqrt(V_j))

# 4. Compute Pr(Best) for each school
best_indices = np.argmax(theta_draws, axis=1)
pr_best = np.bincount(best_indices, minlength=J) / n_samples

for school_idx, prob in enumerate(pr_best):
    print(f"School {chr(65 + school_idx)}: Pr(Best) = {prob:.3f}")

5. Part (b): The No-Pooling Alternative ($\tau = \infty$)

In the simpler model, we assume schools are completely independent ($\tau = \infty$). This is equivalent to saying there is no common population binding them; we estimate each school's effect $\theta_j$ purely from its own sample data:

$$\theta_j \mid y \sim N(y_j, \sigma_j^2) \quad \text{independently}$$

Because the distributions are independent normals, the pairwise probability that School $i$ is better than School $j$ can be calculated analytically:

$$\text{Pr}(\theta_i > \theta_j \mid y) = \Phi\left(\frac{y_i - y_j}{\sqrt{\sigma_i^2 + \sigma_j^2}}\right)$$

where $\Phi$ is the standard normal cumulative distribution function (CDF). The probability that any school is the absolute best is calculated via a single-dimensional numerical integral.

6. Part (c) & (d): What the Physics of Shrinkage Tells Us

Part (c): Shrinkage vs. No-Pooling

Compare the two tables. The separate model ($\tau = \infty$) yields extremely bold, overconfident claims. It estimates the probability that School A is the best at 55.6%. Why? Because it takes School A's measured $+28$ points entirely at face value.

But when we allow the schools to speak to each other under the hierarchical model, we realize that $+28$ is highly likely an outlier inflated by noise ($\sigma_A = 15$). The group estimates shrink toward the population mean $\hat{\mu} \approx 7.7$. Under the full model, School A's probability of being the best drops to 25%, and School G (which has a slightly smaller estimate $+18$ but less noise $\sigma_G = 10$) remains a close competitor with a 21% probability of being the best.

The hierarchical model's conservative results are a much more realistic reflection of the truth: the data suggests coaching effects are relatively similar across schools.

The Counterintuitive Swap: School E vs. School C

Something fascinating happens when we compare School E and School C. Let's look at their raw data:

  • School E: $y_E = -1$, $\sigma_E = 9$ (very little noise)
  • School C: $y_C = -3$, $\sigma_C = 16$ (highly noisy)

Under the separate model ($\tau = \infty$), School E is cleaner and slightly higher than C ($-1 > -3$), so the probability that School E is better than School C is 0.54 (E beats C).

Under the hierarchical model, however, the probability that School C is better than School E rises to 0.58 (C beats E)! Why? Because School C's measurement is highly noisy ($\sigma_C = 16$), so it gets pulled heavily toward the positive population average ($\hat{\mu} \approx 7.7$). School E, having low noise ($\sigma_E = 9$), resists shrinkage and stays closer to its measured negative value of $-1$. Thus, the posterior estimate for C becomes larger than E. This is the power of the model: it understands that a noisy negative measurement is more likely to be a fluke than a precise negative one.

Part (d): Complete Pooling ($\tau = 0$)

If we set the between-school standard deviation $\tau$ to exactly $0$, we assume that all coaching programs are absolutely identical:

$$\theta_1 = \theta_2 = \dots = \theta_8 = \mu$$

In this limit, there is no variation between schools. Consequently:

  • The probability that any school $j$ is the best of the eight is degenerate and equal: $\text{Pr}(\text{best}) = \frac{1}{8} = 12.5\%$.
  • The pairwise probabilities are also degenerate: $\text{Pr}(\theta_i > \theta_j \mid y) = 0.5$ (or undefined if we treat them as strictly identical values, but practically there is no difference between them).
