5.5 Example: Parallel Experiments in Eight Schools

Illustrating the hierarchical normal model with a study of coaching program effects on test scores across eight high schools.

We illustrate the hierarchical normal model with a problem in which the Bayesian analysis gives conclusions that differ in important respects from other methods.

A study was performed for the Educational Testing Service to analyze the effects of special coaching programs on test scores. Separate randomized experiments were performed to estimate the effects of coaching programs for the SAT-V (Scholastic Aptitude Test-Verbal) in each of eight high schools. The outcome variable in each study was the score on a special administration of the SAT-V, a standardized multiple choice test administered by the Educational Testing Service and used to help colleges make admissions decisions; the scores can vary between 200 and 800, with mean about 500 and standard deviation about 100. The SAT examinations are designed to be resistant to short-term efforts directed specifically toward improving performance on the test; instead they are designed to reflect knowledge acquired and abilities developed over many years of education. Nevertheless, each of the eight schools in this study considered its short-term coaching program to be successful at increasing SAT scores. Also, there was no prior reason to believe that any of the eight programs was more effective than any other or that some were more similar in effect to each other than to any other.

The results of the experiments are summarized in Table 5.2. All students in the experiments had already taken the PSAT (Preliminary SAT), and allowance was made for differences in the PSAT-M (Mathematics) and PSAT-V test scores between coached and uncoached students. In particular, in each school the estimated coaching effect and its standard error were obtained by an analysis of covariance adjustment (that is, a linear regression was performed of SAT-V on treatment group, using PSAT-M and PSAT-V as control variables) appropriate for a completely randomized experiment. A separate regression was estimated for each school. Although not simple sample means (because of the covariance adjustments), the estimated coaching effects, which we label $y_j$, and their sampling variances, $\sigma_j^2$, play the same role in our model as $\bar{y}_{\cdot j}$ and $\sigma_j^2$ in the previous section. The estimates $y_j$ are obtained by independent experiments and have approximately normal sampling distributions with sampling variances that are known, for all practical purposes, because the sample sizes in all of the eight experiments were relatively large, over thirty students in each school (recall the discussion of data reduction in Section 4.1). Incidentally, an increase of eight points on the SAT-V corresponds to about one more test item correct.

Feynman's Guide: What is "Analysis of Covariance" (ANCOVA)? Click to toggle explanation ↓

Imagine you want to test if a new pair of running shoes (our coaching program) makes kids run faster. You take a group of kids, give half of them the new shoes, and leave the other half in their old sneakers. Then you measure their times in a final race (the SAT score).

But there's a catch. What if, just by sheer luck, the group that got the new shoes happened to have the fastest kids in the school to begin with? If you just compare the average times of the two groups, you'll think the shoes are magic, when really you're just measuring the kids' natural talent.

To fix this, you look at their times in a practice race before they got the new shoes (the PSAT score). By subtracting their baseline practice times, you "level the starting line." You adjust everyone's final score based on where they started, so you only measure the extra speed added by the shoes themselves. This adjustment is what statisticians call Analysis of Covariance (ANCOVA):

$$\text{Final SAT}_i = \text{Baseline Score} + \text{Extra Speed from Coaching}_i + \text{Unexplained Noise}_i$$
Why do we do this? Because each school only has about 30 kids. With a group that small, a random coin toss can easily put all the high-performing kids into the coached group. Controlling for pre-treatment PSAT scores acts as a correction factor for this baseline imbalance.
What does it achieve?
  • Fairness (No Bias): It lets us compare coached and uncoached students who started at the exact same academic baseline.
  • Precision (Narrower Margins): Since prior test scores are highly predictive of final scores, removing this baseline variation filters out a massive amount of "student-to-student noise." This leaves us with a much cleaner estimate and a significantly smaller margin of error ($\sigma_j$).

Inferences based on nonhierarchical models and their problems

Before fitting the hierarchical Bayesian model, we first consider two simpler nonhierarchical methods—estimating the effects from the eight experiments independently, and complete pooling—and discuss why neither of these approaches is adequate for this example.

Separate estimates

A cursory examination of Table 5.2 may at first suggest that some coaching programs have moderate effects (in the range 18–28 points), most have small effects (0–12 points), and two have small negative effects; however, when we take note of the standard errors of these estimated effects, we see that it is difficult statistically to distinguish between any of the experiments. For example, treating each experiment separately and applying the simple normal analysis in each yields 95% posterior intervals that all overlap substantially.

Separate Estimates with 95% Posterior Intervals

This plot shows the independent estimates ($y_j$) and their 95% uncertainty intervals ($y_j \pm 1.96\sigma_j$) for each school. The vertical dashed line and light blue shaded band represent the completely pooled estimate ($7.7 \pm 8.0$). Note how all the intervals overlap substantially, making it statistically impossible to distinguish the schools based on separate estimates alone.

A pooled estimate

The general overlap in the posterior intervals based on independent analyses suggests that all experiments might be estimating the same quantity. Under the hypothesis that all experiments have the same effect and produce independent estimates of this common effect, we could treat the data in Table 5.2 as eight normally distributed observations with known variances. With a noninformative prior distribution, the posterior mean for the common coaching effect in the schools is $\bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot}$, as defined in equation (5.13) with $y_j$ in place of $\bar{y}_{\cdot j}$:

Common-Effect Estimation (Complete Pooling)

Under complete pooling, the shared effect estimate $\bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot}$ and its variance are:

$$\bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot} = \frac{\sum_{j=1}^8 \frac{y_j}{\sigma_j^2}}{\sum_{j=1}^8 \frac{1}{\sigma_j^2}} = 7.7$$ $$\text{Var}(\bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot}) = \left( \sum_{j=1}^8 \frac{1}{\sigma_j^2} \right)^{-1} = 16.6$$

This yields a posterior standard error of $\sqrt{16.6} \approx 4.1$, leading to a 95% posterior interval of $[-0.5, 15.9]$, or approximately $[8 \pm 8]$.

Supporting this analysis, the classical test of the hypothesis that all $\theta_j$'s are estimating the same quantity yields a $\chi^2$ statistic less than its degrees of freedom ($7$, in this case): $$\sum_{j=1}^8 \frac{(y_j - \bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot})^2}{\sigma_j^2} = 4.6$$ To put it another way, the classical estimate $\hat{\tau}^2$ of the between-school variance $\tau^2$ (from equation 5.22 in Section 5.4) is negative, suggesting no evidence of variance between schools.

How Can a Variance Estimate ($\hat{\tau}^2$) Be Negative? Click to toggle explanation ↓

Before explaining the negative value, let's define what these symbols mean:

  • $\tau$ (tau): The true standard deviation of the treatment effects between schools. It measures the true variation (heterogeneity) among the schools.
  • $\tau^2$: The true variance between schools.
  • $\hat{\tau}^2$: The classical sample estimate of this variance, calculated from our observed data.

A variance tells you how "spread out" things are. Since it is calculated by squaring numbers, true physical variance can never be negative ($\tau^2 \ge 0$). Yet, the mathematical formula for our estimate ($\hat{\tau}^2$) gives us a negative number. Here is why that happens:

Imagine you have eight identical copper coins, all weighing exactly 10 grams. If you weigh them on a scale that has some random measurement noise (sampling error), your readings will fluctuate—say, between 9.8 and 10.2 grams.

If you try to calculate the true differences between the coins, you have to subtract the scale's noise from the total variation you saw:

$$\text{True Variation between Coins } (\tau^2) = \text{Observed Variation of Readings} - \text{Expected Noise of the Scale}$$

Because the coins are actually identical, the observed variation in your readings is entirely due to the scale's noise. In fact, by pure chance, the readings might be clustered even closer together than the scale's average error margin. When you subtract the large expected noise from the small observed variation, the math forces the estimate to go negative.

Visualizing Variance Subtraction

In our SAT study, the schools' estimated coaching effects are so close to each other relative to the very large standard errors of the measurements that the formula concludes there is zero (or mathematically "negative") true variation between the schools.

How we solve this: In classical statistics, we simply round this negative result to zero ($\hat{\tau}^2 = 0$) and assume all schools are identical. But in Bayesian hierarchical modeling, we treat the variance as a probability distribution constrained to be positive. The model peaks at zero but leaves a tail of positive possibilities open, allowing the data to speak for itself without arbitrary truncation.

Would it be possible to see an effect of 28 by chance?

Would it be possible to have one school’s observed effect be 28 just by chance, if the coaching effects in all eight schools were really the same? To get a feeling for the natural variation that we would expect across eight studies if this assumption were true, suppose the estimated treatment effects are eight independent draws from a normal distribution with mean 8 points and standard deviation 13 points (the square root of the mean of the eight variances $\sigma_j^2$).

Then, based on the expected values of normal order statistics, we would expect the largest observed value of $y_j$ to be about 26 points and the others, in diminishing order, to be about 19, 14, 10, 6, 2, $-3$, and $-9$ points. These expected effect sizes are consistent with the set of observed effect sizes in Table 5.2. Thus, it would appear imprudent to believe that school A really has an effect as large as 28 points.

Difficulties with the separate and pooled estimates

To see the problems with the two extreme attitudes—the separate analyses that consider each $\theta_j$ separately, and the alternative view (a single common effect) that leads to the pooled estimate—consider $\theta_1$, the effect in school A.

The effect in school A is estimated as $28.4$ with a standard error of $14.9$ under the separate analysis, versus a pooled estimate of $7.7$ with a standard error of $4.1$ under the common-effect model.

The Limitations of Extremes: Why Both Models Fail

To see the danger of absolute thinking, let's critique the two extremes. Under the separate analysis, the estimate for school A is $28.4 \pm 14.9$. Under complete pooling, it is $7.7 \pm 4.1$. Both models make claims that are scientifically hard to justify.

1. Separate (No Pooling) is Too Noisy

The claim: There is a 50% probability that the true coaching effect in School A ($\theta_A$) is even greater than the observed $28.4$ points.

Why it's wrong: It treats School A in total isolation, ignoring the other seven schools. Since they all cluster much lower (pooled average of 7.7), School A's high score is almost certainly a positive statistical fluke. Ignoring this context leads to excessive optimism.

2. Complete Pooling is Too Rigid

The claim: There is a 50% probability that School A's true effect is less than the overall average of $7.7$ points.

Why it's wrong: It wipes out local data entirely. Even if School A got lucky, an observed 28-point boost is strong evidence that School A's true effect is above average. Forcing it down to 7.7 is too conservative and ignores real signal.

3. The Relational Paradox

The claim: There is a 50% probability that School C (observed effect of $-3$) actually has a larger true coaching effect than School A (observed effect of $+28$).

Why it's wrong: Complete pooling forces all schools to be identical ($\theta_A = \theta_C = 7.7$). This results in the absurd conclusion that the last-place school and first-place school are equally likely to be superior, contradicting physical reality.

Neither estimate is fully satisfactory, and we would like a compromise that combines information from all eight experiments without assuming all the $\theta_j$’s to be equal. The Bayesian analysis under the hierarchical model provides exactly that.

Posterior simulation under the hierarchical model

Consequently, we compute the posterior distribution of $\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_8$, based on the normal model presented in Section 5.4. (More discussion of the reasonableness of applying this model in this problem appears in Sections 6.5 and 17.4.) We draw from the posterior distribution for the Bayesian model by simulating the random variables $\tau$, $\mu$, and $\theta$, in that order, from their posterior distribution, as discussed at the end of the previous section. The sampling standard deviations, $\sigma_j$, are assumed known and equal to the values in Table 5.2, and we assume independent uniform prior densities on $\mu$ and $\tau$.

Results

The marginal posterior density function, $p(\tau \mid y)$ from equation (5.21), is plotted in Figure 5.5. Values of $\tau$ near zero are most plausible; zero is the most likely value, values of $\tau$ larger than 10 are less than half as likely as $\tau = 0$, and $Pr(\tau > 25) \approx 0$. Inference regarding the marginal distributions of the other model parameters and the joint distribution are obtained from the simulated values. In the normal hierarchical model, however, we learn a great deal by considering the conditional posterior distributions given $\tau$ (and averaged over $\mu$).

The marginal posterior density $p(\tau \mid y)$ for the standard deviation of the population of school effects $\theta_j$.

Why Do the Trajectories Cross? (The Shrinkage Paradox) Click to toggle explanation ↓

In Figure 5.6, you will notice that the line for School C (dashed orange line) crosses the lines for School E (purple) and School F (pink) as $\tau$ decreases toward 0. This seems counter-intuitive at first: if School C started with the lowest observed estimate ($-3.0$) and School E started higher ($-0.6$), how does School C end up above School E at intermediate values of $\tau$?

The answer lies in the measurement precision (the standard errors $\sigma_j$ in Table 5.2):

  • School C: Observed effect of $-3.0$ but with a very large standard error of $16.3$. The model has very little confidence in this number.
  • School E: Observed effect of $-0.6$ with a much smaller standard error of $8.5$. The model has relatively high confidence in this number.

When the between-school standard deviation $\tau$ is small, the model forces all estimates to pool strongly toward the overall average of $7.7$.

Because School C's data is so noisy (large standard error), the model discounts it rapidly, pulling its estimate toward the pooled average of $7.7$ very quickly. On the other hand, because School E's estimate is much more precise, the model resists pooling it, allowing its estimate to stick closer to its observed value of $-0.6$ for longer.

As we decrease $\tau$, School C zooms upward toward $7.7$ much faster than School E does, crossing it in the process. This is the hallmark of Bayesian shrinkage: noisy estimates shrink faster than precise ones.

The lines in Figure 5.7 show the conditional standard deviations, $sd(\theta_j \mid \tau, y)$, as a function of $\tau$. As $\tau$ increases, the population distribution allows the eight effects to be more different from each other, and hence the posterior uncertainty in each individual $\theta_j$ increases, approaching the standard deviations in Table 5.2 in the limit of $\tau \to \infty$. (The posterior means and standard deviations for the components $\theta_j$, given $\tau$, are computed using the mean and variance formulas (2.7) and (2.8), averaging over $\mu$; see Exercise 5.12.)

Conditional posterior standard deviations $sd(\theta_j \mid \tau, y)$ as functions of the between-school standard deviation $\tau$.

The general conclusion from an examination of Figures 5.5–5.7 is that an effect as large as 28.4 points in any school is unlikely. For the likely values of $\tau$, the estimates in all schools are substantially less than 28 points. For example, even at $\tau = 10$, the probability that the effect in school A is less than 28 points is $\Phi[(28 - 14.5)/9.1] = 93\%$, where $\Phi$ is the standard normal cumulative distribution function; the corresponding probabilities for the effects being less than 28 points in the other schools are 99.5%, 99.2%, 98.5%, 99.96%, 99.8%, 97%, and 98%.

Of substantial importance, we do not obtain an accurate summary of the data if we condition on the posterior mode of $\tau$. The technique of conditioning on a modal value (for example, the maximum likelihood estimate) of a hyperparameter such as $\tau$ is often used in practice (at least as an approximation), but it ignores the uncertainty conveyed by the posterior distribution of the hyperparameter. At $\tau = 0$, the inference is that all experiments have the same size effect, 7.7 points, and the same standard error, 4.1 points. Figures 5.5–5.7 certainly suggest that this answer represents too much pulling together of the estimates in the eight schools. The problem is especially acute in this example because the posterior mode of $\tau$ is on the boundary of its parameter space. A joint posterior modal estimate of $(\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_J, \mu, \tau)$ suffers from even worse problems in general.

Discussion

Table 5.3 summarizes the 200 simulated effect estimates for all eight schools. In one sense, these results are similar to the pooled 95% interval $[8 \pm 8]$, in that the eight Bayesian 95% intervals largely overlap and are median-centered between 5 and 10. In a second sense, the results in the table differ from the pooled estimate in a direction toward the eight independent answers: the 95% Bayesian intervals are each almost twice as wide as the one common interval and suggest substantially greater probabilities of effects larger than 16 points, especially in school A, and greater probabilities of negative effects, especially in school C. If greater precision were required in the posterior intervals, one could simulate more simulation draws; we use only 200 draws here to illustrate that a small simulation gives adequate inference for many practical purposes.

However, there are differences in the details; for example, the Bayesian probability that the effect in school A is as large as 28 points is less than 10%, which is substantially less than the 50% probability based on the separate estimate for school A.

As an illustration of the simulation-based posterior results, 200 simulations of school A’s effect are shown in Figure 5.8a. Having simulated the parameter $\theta$, it is easy to ask more complicated questions of this model. For example, what is the posterior distribution of $\max\{\theta_j\}$, the effect of the most successful of the eight coaching programs? Figure 5.8b displays a histogram of 200 values from this posterior distribution and shows that only 22 draws are larger than 28.4; thus, $Pr(\max\{\theta_j\} > 28.4) \approx 22/200 = 11\%$. Since Figure 5.8a gives the marginal posterior distribution of the effect in school A, and Figure 5.8b gives the marginal posterior distribution of the largest effect no matter which school it is in, the latter figure has larger values. For another example, we can estimate $Pr(\theta_1 > \theta_3 \mid y)$, the posterior probability that the coaching program is more effective in school A than in school C, by the proportion of simulated draws of $\theta$ for which $\theta_1 > \theta_3$; the result is $141/200 = 0.705$.

To sum up, the Bayesian analysis of this example not only allows straightforward inferences about many parameters that may be of interest, but the hierarchical model is flexible enough to adapt to the data, thereby providing posterior inferences that account for the partial pooling as well as the uncertainty in the hyperparameters.
