5.5 Example: Parallel Experiments in Eight Schools

Illustrating the hierarchical normal model with a study of coaching program effects on test scores across eight high schools.

Reading Mode:

We illustrate the hierarchical normal model with a problem in which the Bayesian analysis gives conclusions that differ in important respects from other methods.

A study was performed for the Educational Testing Service to analyze the effects of special coaching programs on test scores. Separate randomized experiments were performed to estimate the effects of coaching programs for the SAT-V (Scholastic Aptitude Test-Verbal) in each of eight high schools. The outcome variable in each study was the score on a special administration of the SAT-V, a standardized multiple choice test administered by the Educational Testing Service and used to help colleges make admissions decisions; the scores can vary between 200 and 800, with mean about 500 and standard deviation about 100. The SAT examinations are designed to be resistant to short-term efforts directed specifically toward improving performance on the test; instead they are designed to reflect knowledge acquired and abilities developed over many years of education. Nevertheless, each of the eight schools in this study considered its short-term coaching program to be successful at increasing SAT scores. Also, there was no prior reason to believe that any of the eight programs was more effective than any other or that some were more similar in effect to each other than to any other.

The results of the experiments are summarized in Table 5.2. All students in the experiments had already taken the PSAT (Preliminary SAT), and allowance was made for differences in the PSAT-M (Mathematics) and PSAT-V test scores between coached and uncoached students. In particular, in each school the estimated coaching effect and its standard error were obtained by an analysis of covariance adjustment (that is, a linear regression was performed of SAT-V on treatment group, using PSAT-M and PSAT-V as control variables) appropriate for a completely randomized experiment. A separate regression was estimated for each school. Although not simple sample means (because of the covariance adjustments), the estimated coaching effects, which we label $y_j$, and their sampling variances, $\sigma_j^2$, play the same role in our model as $\bar{y}_{\cdot j}$ and $\sigma_j^2$ in the previous section. The estimates $y_j$ are obtained by independent experiments and have approximately normal sampling distributions with sampling variances that are known, for all practical purposes, because the sample sizes in all of the eight experiments were relatively large, over thirty students in each school (recall the discussion of data reduction in Section 4.1). Incidentally, an increase of eight points on the SAT-V corresponds to about one more test item correct.

School Estimated treatment effect, $y_j$ Standard error estimate, $\sigma_j$
A 28 15
B 8 10
C -3 16
D 7 11
E -1 9
F 1 11
G 18 10
H 12 18
Table 5.2: Observed effects of special preparation on SAT-V scores in eight randomized experiments. Estimates are based on separate covariance analyses for each high school.

Inferences based on nonhierarchical models and their problems

Before fitting the hierarchical Bayesian model, we first consider two simpler nonhierarchical methods—estimating the effects from the eight experiments independently, and complete pooling—and discuss why neither of these approaches is adequate for this example.

Separate estimates

A cursory examination of Table 5.2 may at first suggest that some coaching programs have moderate effects (in the range 18–28 points), most have small effects (0–12 points), and two have small negative effects; however, when we take note of the standard errors of these estimated effects, we see that it is difficult statistically to distinguish between any of the experiments. For example, treating each experiment separately and applying the simple normal analysis in each yields 95% posterior intervals that all overlap substantially.

A pooled estimate

The general overlap in the posterior intervals based on independent analyses suggests that all experiments might be estimating the same quantity. Under the hypothesis that all experiments have the same effect and produce independent estimates of this common effect, we could treat the data in Table 5.2 as eight normally distributed observations with known variances. With a noninformative prior distribution, the posterior mean for the common coaching effect in the schools is $\bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot}$, as defined in equation (5.13) with $y_j$ in place of $\bar{y}_{\cdot j}$:

Common-Effect Estimation (Complete Pooling)

Under complete pooling, the shared effect estimate $\bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot}$ and its variance are:

$$\bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot} = \frac{\sum_{j=1}^8 \frac{y_j}{\sigma_j^2}}{\sum_{j=1}^8 \frac{1}{\sigma_j^2}} = 7.7$$ $$\text{Var}(\bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot}) = \left( \sum_{j=1}^8 \frac{1}{\sigma_j^2} \right)^{-1} = 16.6$$

This yields a posterior standard error of $\sqrt{16.6} \approx 4.1$, leading to a 95% posterior interval of $[-0.5, 15.9]$, or approximately $[8 \pm 8]$.

Supporting this analysis, the classical test of the hypothesis that all $\theta_j$'s are estimating the same quantity yields a $\chi^2$ statistic less than its degrees of freedom ($7$, in this case): $$\sum_{j=1}^8 \frac{(y_j - \bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot})^2}{\sigma_j^2} = 4.6$$ To put it another way, the estimate $\hat{\tau}^2$ from equation (5.22) is negative, suggesting no evidence of variance between schools.

Would it be possible to see an effect of 28 by chance?

Would it be possible to have one school’s observed effect be 28 just by chance, if the coaching effects in all eight schools were really the same? To get a feeling for the natural variation that we would expect across eight studies if this assumption were true, suppose the estimated treatment effects are eight independent draws from a normal distribution with mean 8 points and standard deviation 13 points (the square root of the mean of the eight variances $\sigma_j^2$).

Then, based on the expected values of normal order statistics, we would expect the largest observed value of $y_j$ to be about 26 points and the others, in diminishing order, to be about 19, 14, 10, 6, 2, $-3$, and $-9$ points. These expected effect sizes are consistent with the set of observed effect sizes in Table 5.2. Thus, it would appear imprudent to believe that school A really has an effect as large as 28 points.

Difficulties with the separate and pooled estimates

To see the problems with the two extreme attitudes—the separate analyses that consider each $\theta_j$ separately, and the alternative view (a single common effect) that leads to the pooled estimate—consider $\theta_1$, the effect in school A.

The effect in school A is estimated as $28.4$ with a standard error of $14.9$ under the separate analysis, versus a pooled estimate of $7.7$ with a standard error of $4.1$ under the common-effect model.

The Limitations of Extremes

  • The separate analyses of the eight schools imply: "the probability is $\frac{1}{2}$ that the true effect in A is more than $28.4$", a doubtful statement, considering the results for the other seven schools.
  • On the other hand, the pooled model implies: "the probability is $\frac{1}{2}$ that the true effect in A is less than $7.7$", which, despite the non-significant $\chi^2$ test, seems an inaccurate summary of our knowledge.
  • The pooled model also implies: "the probability is $\frac{1}{2}$ that the true effect in A is less than the true effect in C", which is also difficult to justify given the data in Table 5.2.

Neither estimate is fully satisfactory, and we would like a compromise that combines information from all eight experiments without assuming all the $\theta_j$’s to be equal. The Bayesian analysis under the hierarchical model provides exactly that.

Now, we're going to look at a problem where ordinary statistics falls flat on its face. You see, if you use standard methods, you get one answer. But if you think like a Bayesian, and look at the whole picture, you get a completely different, much more sensible conclusion. Let me show you how this works.

Let’s start with the real-world setup. We have this organization called the Educational Testing Service. They wanted to know: does short-term coaching actually help kids do better on the SAT? Now, the SAT is designed to be tough to beat. It's supposed to measure abilities built over years. But all these eight different high schools ran their own coaching programs, and every single school was convinced their program was a huge success.

But here's the catch: they had no prior reason to think any one school's program was better than the others. To them, they were all just schools trying their best, completely independent, with no obvious differences in quality. So they ran randomized experiments in each school. They collected the data, adjusted for the kids' starting scores (using covariance, which is just a fancy way of leveling the playing field), and came up with an estimated coaching effect for each school, which we'll call $y_j$, and a measure of the noise, which we'll call the standard error $\sigma_j$.

Let's Try to Solve it Without Hierarchies (And Watch it Fail)

Before we do the smart Bayesian compromise, let's try the two simple nonhierarchical paths that most people jump to. We can analyze all the experiments independently, or we can pool them completely. Let's see why both of these approaches are completely inadequate.

Separate estimates: The isolation trap

If you take a quick look at the raw table, you might think: 'Look! Some coaching programs work well (School A got 28 points, School G got 18), some have tiny effects, and some are negative.' But hold on! Look at the standard errors. When you calculate the 95% posterior interval for each school separately, they all overlap substantially. School A is $28 \pm 30$, School C is $-3 \pm 32$. Because the noise is so large, you cannot statistically tell any of these schools apart. They are all stepping on each other's toes in terms of error bars. If you treat them separately, you're pretending they're isolated islands, but your data is too weak to back that up.

Complete pooling: The clone assumption

Since all the school intervals overlap, you might think: 'Why don't we assume all these schools are identical?' E.g. they are all estimating the exact same underlying effect. Under this assumption, we treat the data as eight independent observations of a single shared parameter. Using a noninformative prior, we calculate the weighted average of the effects, where the weights are based on the precision (the inverse of variance) of each experiment:

Common-Effect Estimation (Complete Pooling)

We combine all the experiments using their inverse variances as weights:

$$\bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot} = \frac{\sum_{j=1}^8 \frac{y_j}{\sigma_j^2}}{\sum_{j=1}^8 \frac{1}{\sigma_j^2}} = 7.7$$ $$\text{Var}(\bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot}) = \left( \sum_{j=1}^8 \frac{1}{\sigma_j^2} \right)^{-1} = 16.6$$

This yields a pooled estimate of 7.7 with a standard error of $\sqrt{16.6} \approx 4.1$, leading to a 95% posterior interval of approximately $[8 \pm 8]$.

If you run a classical hypothesis test on this, checking if all the schools' true effects are equal, the test statistic is: $$\sum_{j=1}^8 \frac{(y_j - \bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot})^2}{\sigma_j^2} = 4.6$$ Which is 4.6. For 7 degrees of freedom, that is tiny! It suggests there's no statistical evidence of variation between the schools. The estimator for the school-to-school variance $\hat{\tau}^2$ actually comes out negative!

Would it be possible to see an effect of 28 by chance?

But wait, could we really get an observed effect of 28 in school A just by chance if all the schools were identical? Let's run a simple simulation in our heads. Suppose the true coaching effect in all eight schools is exactly 8 points, and we draw eight observations with standard errors of 13 points (the average noise across our experiments). If you draw eight random samples from a normal distribution with mean 8 and standard deviation 13, what would you expect the order of those draws to be?

Using the math of normal order statistics, you'd expect the largest draw to be about 26 points, and the rest to fall in order around 19, 14, 10, 6, 2, $-3$, and $-9$ points. Now look at our actual table of results: 28, 18, 12, 8, 7, 1, $-1$, and $-3$. They match almost perfectly! So, yes! An observed effect of 28 in school A is completely consistent with pure random noise. It would be foolish to believe School A has a program that is uniquely superior.

The paradox of extremes

So we have two extreme choices, and both feel broken: Let's focus on School A's true effect, $\theta_1$.

The Limitations of Extremes

  • The separate analysis says: "The best guess for School A is 28.4, and the probability is 50% that the true effect is greater than 28.4." But this is implausible when you look at all the other schools.
  • The complete pooling model says: "The best guess for School A is 7.7, and the probability is 50% that it is less than 7.7." But this feels unreasonable when School A's raw estimate was 28!
  • The pooled model also claims: "There is a 50% chance that School A's true effect is worse than School C's true effect." Even though School C's raw estimate was -3! This is hard to justify.

Neither estimate is satisfying. We want a compromise. A model that allows the schools to have different true effects, but pulls them toward a common average based on how noisy they are. That's the magic of hierarchical Bayesian modeling. It lets the schools talk to each other, sharing strength, without forcing them to be identical clones.
