5.3 Fully Bayesian Analysis of Conjugate Hierarchical Models

Overcoming point-estimation limitations by putting a probability model on the hyperparameters themselves.

The Fully Bayesian Philosophy

In the previous section, we explored an Empirical Bayes approach: we calculated the empirical mean and standard deviation of historical rat tumor rates, and "plugged" those values in to determine a fixed Beta prior distribution.

While mathematically convenient, that approach has a fatal logical flaw: it ignores our uncertainty regarding the population hyperparameters \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\). By treating them as absolute, known constants, we artificially inflate our posterior precision and "use our data twice" (once to construct the prior, and once to update it).

To remain theoretically consistent and structurally rigorous, we must treat the hyperparameters \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) as random variables. This requires setting up a joint prior probability distribution, \(p(\alpha, \beta)\)—referred to as a hyperprior—and computing the joint posterior distribution for all parameters simultaneously.

The Probability Model

Let \(y_j\) be the number of tumors observed in the \(j\)-th experiment, with sample size \(n_j\), for \(j = 1, \dots, J\). We assume a Binomial likelihood for each group:

\(y_j \mid \theta_j \sim \text{Binomial}(n_j, \theta_j)\)

The local parameters \(\theta_j\) are independent draws from a shared population-level prior:

\(\theta_j \mid \alpha, \beta \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)\)

In a fully Bayesian setup, we define a joint hyperprior distribution \(p(\alpha, \beta)\). The full joint posterior distribution of all parameters is given by:

\(p(\theta, \alpha, \beta \mid y) \propto p(\alpha, \beta) p(\theta \mid \alpha, \beta) p(y \mid \theta, \alpha, \beta)\)

We can expand the terms \(p(\theta \mid \alpha, \beta) p(y \mid \theta, \alpha, \beta)\) by substituting the probability density function of the Beta distribution and the probability mass function of the Binomial distribution:

$$ \begin{aligned} p(\theta \mid \alpha, \beta) p(y \mid \theta, \alpha, \beta) &= \prod_{j=1}^J p(\theta_j \mid \alpha, \beta) p(y_j \mid \theta_j, \alpha, \beta) \\ &= \prod_{j=1}^J \left[ \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)} \theta_j^{\alpha-1}(1-\theta_j)^{\beta-1} \right] \left[ \binom{n_j}{y_j} \theta_j^{y_j}(1-\theta_j)^{n_j-y_j} \right] \\ &= \prod_{j=1}^J \binom{n_j}{y_j} \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)} \theta_j^{\alpha + y_j - 1} (1-\theta_j)^{\beta + n_j - y_j - 1} \end{aligned} $$

Combining these, we obtain the total formula for the joint posterior distribution \(p(\theta, \alpha, \beta \mid y)\). Since the binomial coefficient \(\binom{n_j}{y_j}\) is a constant with respect to all the model parameters, we can omit it to express the standard proportional relation:

$$p(\theta, \alpha, \beta \mid y) \propto p(\alpha, \beta) \prod_{j=1}^J \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)} \theta_j^{\alpha + y_j - 1} (1-\theta_j)^{\beta + n_j - y_j - 1}$$

Specifying the Hyperprior

To perform a fully Bayesian hierarchical analysis, we must specify a joint prior distribution \(p(\alpha, \beta)\) for the hyperparameters. Choosing a naive flat prior directly on the original scale, such as \(p(\alpha, \beta) \propto 1\), is highly problematic: if any individual experiment has 0 successes or 0 failures (which is common in sparse datasets like the rat tumor study), the tail of the posterior distribution will not decay, rendering the final posterior distribution improper (its integral over the parameter space diverges to infinity).

To solve this, Gelman et al. construct a prior based on the physical interpretation of the population Beta distribution. We can parameterize the prior distribution by its location (prior mean) and its scale (which relates directly to the prior standard deviation):

  • Prior Mean (\(\mu\)): \(\mu = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha+\beta}\), representing where the population rate is centered on the interval \([0, 1]\).
  • Prior Scale (\(\eta\)): \(\eta = (\alpha+\beta)^{-1/2}\), which represents the prior standard deviation (when \(\mu\) is held constant, the standard deviation is proportional to \((\alpha+\beta)^{-1/2}\)).

A standard, objective way to express complete relative ignorance about these two decoupled aspects is to assign a joint flat (uniform) prior to them:

Diffuse Hyperprior in the Location-Scale Space

\(p(\mu, \eta) \propto 1\)

This reflects a uniform distribution over the prior mean \(\mu \in [0, 1]\) and the precision-scale \(\eta \in [0, \infty)\).

To translate this uniform prior back to the original hyperparameters \((\alpha, \beta)\), we must apply the multivariate change of variables formula using the Jacobian determinant:

Show Complete Mathematical Proof of the Hyperprior Jacobian Derivation

The change of variables theorem states that: $$p(\alpha, \beta) = p(\mu(\alpha, \beta), \eta(\alpha, \beta)) \times |J|$$ Since the prior is uniform on the transformed coordinates, \(p(\mu, \eta) \propto 1\), the density is simply proportional to the absolute determinant of the Jacobian matrix: $$p(\alpha, \beta) \propto \left| \det \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial \mu}{\partial \alpha} & \frac{\partial \mu}{\partial \beta} \\ \frac{\partial \eta}{\partial \alpha} & \frac{\partial \eta}{\partial \beta} \end{pmatrix} \right|$$

1. Computing the Partial Derivatives

First, for the prior mean \(\mu = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha+\beta}\), we apply the quotient rule: $$\frac{\partial \mu}{\partial \alpha} = \frac{1 \cdot (\alpha+\beta) - \alpha \cdot 1}{(\alpha+\beta)^2} = \frac{\beta}{(\alpha+\beta)^2}$$ $$\frac{\partial \mu}{\partial \beta} = \frac{0 \cdot (\alpha+\beta) - \alpha \cdot 1}{(\alpha+\beta)^2} = -\frac{\alpha}{(\alpha+\beta)^2}$$

Second, for the scale parameter \(\eta = (\alpha+\beta)^{-1/2}\), we apply the power and chain rules: $$\frac{\partial \eta}{\partial \alpha} = -\frac{1}{2}(\alpha+\beta)^{-3/2}$$ $$\frac{\partial \eta}{\partial \beta} = -\frac{1}{2}(\alpha+\beta)^{-3/2}$$

2. Computing the Determinant

Plugging these partial derivatives into the determinant formula: $$ \begin{aligned} \det(J) &= \left(\frac{\beta}{(\alpha+\beta)^2}\right)\left(-\frac{1}{2}(\alpha+\beta)^{-3/2}\right) - \left(-\frac{\alpha}{(\alpha+\beta)^2}\right)\left(-\frac{1}{2}(\alpha+\beta)^{-3/2}\right) \\ &= -\frac{1}{2}(\alpha+\beta)^{-3/2} \left[ \frac{\beta}{(\alpha+\beta)^2} + \frac{\alpha}{(\alpha+\beta)^2} \right] \\ &= -\frac{1}{2}(\alpha+\beta)^{-3/2} \left[ \frac{\alpha+\beta}{(\alpha+\beta)^2} \right] \\ &= -\frac{1}{2}(\alpha+\beta)^{-3/2} \left[ \frac{1}{\alpha+\beta} \right] \\ &= -\frac{1}{2}(\alpha+\beta)^{-5/2} \end{aligned} $$

3. Conclusion

Taking the absolute value and dropping the constant multiplier \(\frac{1}{2}\) yields the exact prior density on the original scale: $$p(\alpha, \beta) \propto (\alpha + \beta)^{-5/2}$$ This is Equation 5.9 in BDA3. This power of decay (\(-2.5\)) is critical; it decays quickly enough as \(\alpha+\beta \to \infty\) to guarantee that the posterior integrates to a finite volume, producing a mathematically valid (proper) posterior distribution.

Deriving the Marginal Posterior

To sample from the joint posterior density, we must first compute the marginal posterior density of the hyperparameters, \(p(\alpha, \beta \mid y)\). We can do this by analytically integrating out the local parameters \(\theta\) from the joint posterior density:

$$ \begin{aligned} p(\alpha, \beta \mid y) &= \int p(\theta, \alpha, \beta \mid y) \, d\theta \\ &\propto \int p(\alpha, \beta) \prod_{j=1}^J \left[ \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)} \theta_j^{\alpha+y_j-1} (1-\theta_j)^{\beta+n_j-y_j-1} \right] \, d\theta \\ &= p(\alpha, \beta) \prod_{j=1}^J \int_0^1 \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)} \theta_j^{\alpha+y_j-1} (1-\theta_j)^{\beta+n_j-y_j-1} \, d\theta_j \\ &= p(\alpha, \beta) \prod_{j=1}^J \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)} \left[ \int_0^1 \theta_j^{\alpha+y_j-1} (1-\theta_j)^{\beta+n_j-y_j-1} \, d\theta_j \right] \end{aligned} $$

Notice that the expression inside the integral is the kernel of an unnormalized Beta distribution, specifically \(\text{Beta}(\alpha + y_j, \beta + n_j - y_j)\). Since a Beta probability density function integrates to 1, we know that: $$\int_0^1 \theta_j^{\alpha+y_j-1} (1-\theta_j)^{\beta+n_j-y_j-1} \, d\theta_j = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+y_j)\Gamma(\beta+n_j-y_j)}{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta+n_j)}$$

Substituting this back into the product yields the standard analytical marginal posterior formula:

$$p(\alpha, \beta \mid y) \propto p(\alpha, \beta) \prod_{j=1}^J \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)} \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+y_j)\Gamma(\beta+n_j-y_j)}{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta+n_j)}$$

This is Equation 5.8 in BDA3.

Alternative Intuition: The Beta-Binomial Mixture

We can obtain the exact same result directly through probability rules without performing calculus. Conditional on the hyperparameters \((\alpha, \beta)\), each group parameter \(\theta_j\) has an independent Beta distribution, and the data \(y_j\) has a Binomial likelihood.

This compound model represents a Beta-Binomial distribution for each experiment \(j\). The marginal probability of observing \(y_j\) successes in \(n_j\) trials given hyperparameters \((\alpha, \beta)\) is: $$p(y_j \mid \alpha, \beta) = \binom{n_j}{y_j} \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)} \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+y_j)\Gamma(\beta+n_j-y_j)}{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta+n_j)}$$

Since each experiment is conditionally independent, the joint probability of the entire dataset is the product of these Beta-Binomial probabilities. Multiplying this joint probability by the hyperprior \(p(\alpha, \beta)\) and omitting constant binomial coefficients gives the identical marginal posterior distribution (5.8).

Substituting our derived hyperprior \(p(\alpha, \beta) \propto (\alpha + \beta)^{-5/2}\) completes the analytical specification.

Reparameterization to Unconstrained Space

Although we have the analytical marginal posterior, computing it directly on the positive quadrant \((\alpha > 0, \beta > 0)\) is numerically unstable because \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are highly correlated and span several orders of magnitude.

To make computation and grid plotting highly stable and well-behaved, we perform a second transformation to map the positive parameters to the unconstrained real plane \(\mathbb{R}^2\):

  • Log-odds of the Mean (\(u\)): \(u = \log\left(\frac{\alpha}{\beta}\right)\) — controls the prior location.
  • Log of the Precision (\(v\)): \(v = \log(\alpha + \beta)\) — controls the prior concentration.

To evaluate the posterior density on this grid, we must transform our marginal probability density from \((\alpha, \beta)\) coordinates to the new grid coordinates \((u, v)\). The Jacobian determinant for this log-transformation maps as: $$\left| \det \frac{\partial(u, v)}{\partial(\alpha, \beta)} \right| = \left| \det \begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{\alpha} & -\frac{1}{\beta} \\ \frac{1}{\alpha+\beta} & \frac{1}{\alpha+\beta} \end{pmatrix} \right| = \frac{1}{\alpha\beta}$$

Therefore, the inverse transformation Jacobian determinant to map the density from the original scale to the log-grid scale is the reciprocal, \(\alpha\beta\). Multiplying our density by this factor yields Equation 5.10 from BDA3:

$$p(u, v \mid y) \propto \alpha\beta \, (\alpha + \beta)^{-5/2} \prod_{j=1}^J \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)} \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+y_j)\Gamma(\beta+n_j-y_j)}{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta+n_j)}$$

This is the exact, unconstrained joint posterior distribution that is plotted and evaluated on the grid in our interactive dashboard below.

The Hierarchical Sampling Steps

Once the joint posterior density \(p(\alpha, \beta \mid y)\) is mapped, obtaining fully Bayesian draws from our models is extremely simple and computationally robust:

Step-by-Step Simulation Algorithm

  1. Sample the Hyperparameters: Draw a pair \((\alpha, \beta)\) from the joint marginal posterior distribution \(p(\alpha, \beta \mid y)\) plotted above.
  2. Sample the Group Parameters: For each experiment \(j\), draw a group parameter \(\theta_j\) from its conditionally conjugate Beta posterior distribution:
    \(\theta_j \mid \alpha, \beta, y_j \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha + y_j, \beta + n_j - y_j)\)

Notice how this elegant, two-step structure propagates all hyperparameter uncertainties directly into the local estimations. If \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) have wide margins of error, our individual group estimates will naturally adjust to incorporate that broader range.
