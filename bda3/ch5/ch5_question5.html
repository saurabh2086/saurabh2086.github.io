First-Principles Derivation

Mixtures of Independent Distributions

If a joint prior distribution of parameters is represented as a mixture of independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) components, what does this mathematically imply about the covariance between the parameters? We prove from first principles that the covariance must be non-negative, and explore the Bayesian intuition of why a shared hyperparameter prevents negative correlation.

Exercise Description

Suppose the distribution of $\theta = (\theta_1, \dots, \theta_J)$ can be written as a mixture of independent and identically distributed components: $$p(\theta) = \int \prod_{j=1}^J p(\theta_j \mid \phi) p(\phi) d\phi$$ Prove that the covariances $\text{cov}(\theta_i, \theta_j)$ are all non-negative for $i \neq j$.

1. First-Principles Derivation

We start with the fundamental definition of covariance for any two random variables $\theta_i$ and $\theta_j$ ($i \neq j$):

$$\text{cov}(\theta_i, \theta_j) = E[\theta_i \theta_j] - E[\theta_i]E[\theta_j]$$

Let $\mu_i(\phi) = E[\theta_i \mid \phi]$ be the conditional expectation of $\theta_i$ given the hyperparameter vector $\phi$. Since the components are conditionally identically distributed given $\phi$, their conditional expectations are identical:

$$\mu_i(\phi) = \mu_j(\phi) = \mu(\phi)$$

Now we evaluate the expectation of the product $E[\theta_i \theta_j]$ by conditioning on $\phi$ using the law of total expectation:

$$E[\theta_i \theta_j] = E[E[\theta_i \theta_j \mid \phi]]$$

Because $\theta_i$ and $\theta_j$ are conditionally independent given $\phi$, their joint conditional expectation is the product of their individual conditional expectations:

$$E[\theta_i \theta_j \mid \phi] = E[\theta_i \mid \phi] E[\theta_j \mid \phi] = \mu(\phi)^2$$

Taking the expectation over the hyperparameter prior $p(\phi)$:

$$E[\theta_i \theta_j] = E[\mu(\phi)^2]$$

Similarly, we compute the marginal expectations of the individual parameters:

$$E[\theta_i] = E[E[\theta_i \mid \phi]] = E[\mu(\phi)]$$ $$E[\theta_j] = E[E[\theta_j \mid \phi]] = E[\mu(\phi)]$$

So the product of their marginal expectations is:

$$E[\theta_i] E[\theta_j] = (E[\mu(\phi)])^2$$

Now we substitute these two results back into our covariance formula:

$$\text{cov}(\theta_i, \theta_j) = E[\mu(\phi)^2] - (E[\mu(\phi)])^2$$

This expression is exactly the mathematical definition of the variance of the random variable $X = \mu(\phi)$:

$$\text{cov}(\theta_i, \theta_j) = \text{var}(\mu(\phi))$$

By definition, the variance of any real-valued random variable is non-negative:

$$\text{var}(\mu(\phi)) = E[(\mu(\phi) - E[\mu(\phi)])^2] \ge 0$$
Conclusion of the Proof

The covariance is equivalent to the variance of the conditional expectation under the prior. Since variance must be non-negative, we conclude: $$\text{cov}(\theta_i, \theta_j) \ge 0$$

2. Bayesian Intuition & Information Transfer

This result reveals a deep property of Bayesian hierarchical models.

Imagine we observe a very large value for the parameter $\theta_i$. Because all parameters share the same hyperparameter vector $\phi$, this observation updates our beliefs:

  • Information Transfer: Seeing a large $\theta_i$ shifts our posterior distribution over $\phi$ to favor values that produce larger parameter draws.
  • Positive Co-Movement: Since $\theta_j$ is drawn from the same distribution parameterized by $\phi$, this shift in the distribution of $\phi$ pulls our expectations for $\theta_j$ in the same direction.

The parameters $\theta_i$ and $\theta_j$ are coupled through our uncertainty about $\phi$. The covariance $\text{cov}(\theta_i, \theta_j)$ is exactly the variance of this shared shifting center, $\text{var}(\mu(\phi))$:

  • If we have no uncertainty about $\phi$ (i.e. $\phi$ is a fixed, known constant), then $\mu(\phi)$ is a constant. Its variance is $0$, and the parameters are truly independent: $\text{cov}(\theta_i, \theta_j) = 0$.
  • If we have any uncertainty about $\phi$, that uncertainty acts as a source of positive co-movement. Learning about one parameter always updates our belief about the shared center, pulling our belief about the other parameters in the same direction, generating a positive covariance: $\text{cov}(\theta_i, \theta_j) > 0$.

Because the variables only interact through their shared dependence on $\phi$, it is mathematically impossible for this model to generate negative correlation. To get negative correlation, there must be a global constraint (like a fixed budget or finite selection quota) which is not present in an i.i.d. mixture model.
