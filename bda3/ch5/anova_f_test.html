Classical Random-Effects ANOVA and the F-Test

Deriving the sum of squares partitioning, expected mean squares, and the classical decision rules from first principles.

1. The Classical ANOVA Framework

In classical (non-Bayesian) statistics, Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) is a collection of models and statistical procedures used to analyze the differences among group means. While a standard fixed-effects ANOVA asks whether specific group means are different from each other, a random-effects ANOVA assumes that the groups themselves are a random sample from a larger population.

To make the derivations as simple and clear as possible, we assume a balanced design. This means we have \(J\) independent groups (or experiments), and we collect exactly \(n\) observations from each group, for a total of \(Jn\) observations.

Let \(y_{ij}\) represent the \(i\)-th observation in the \(j\)-th group. The mathematical model is: $$y_{ij} = \mu + \alpha_j + \epsilon_{ij}$$ where:

  • \(\mu\) (Grand Mean): The overall average value of the population.
  • \(\alpha_j\) (Group Effect): The deviation of group \(j\)'s mean from the grand mean, distributed as \(\alpha_j \sim N(0, \tau^2)\).
  • \(\epsilon_{ij}\) (Residual Error): The individual-level deviation of observation \(i\) in group \(j\) from its group mean, distributed as \(\epsilon_{ij} \sim N(0, \sigma^2)\).

Under this formulation, the total variance of any single observation \(y_{ij}\) is the sum of two components: the group-level variance \(\tau^2\) and the individual-level measurement variance \(\sigma^2\): $$\text{var}(y_{ij}) = \tau^2 + \sigma^2$$

2. Partitioning the Sum of Squares

The core idea of ANOVA is to decompose the total variation in the data into variation between groups and variation within groups.

Let \(\bar{y}_{\cdot j}\) be the sample mean of group \(j\), and \(\bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot}\) be the grand mean of all observations: $$\bar{y}_{\cdot j} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n y_{ij}, \quad \bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot} = \frac{1}{Jn} \sum_{j=1}^J \sum_{i=1}^n y_{ij}$$

We can write the total deviation of any observation \(y_{ij}\) from the grand mean as: $$y_{ij} - \bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot} = (\bar{y}_{\cdot j} - \bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot}) + (y_{ij} - \bar{y}_{\cdot j})$$ If we square both sides and sum over all \(i\) and \(j\), we obtain: $$\sum_{j=1}^J \sum_{i=1}^n (y_{ij} - \bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot})^2 = \sum_{j=1}^J \sum_{i=1}^n \left[ (\bar{y}_{\cdot j} - \bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot}) + (y_{ij} - \bar{y}_{\cdot j}) \right]^2$$ $$\sum_{j=1}^J \sum_{i=1}^n (y_{ij} - \bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot})^2 = \sum_{j=1}^J \sum_{i=1}^n (\bar{y}_{\cdot j} - \bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot})^2 + \sum_{j=1}^J \sum_{i=1}^n (y_{ij} - \bar{y}_{\cdot j})^2 + 2 \sum_{j=1}^J \sum_{i=1}^n (\bar{y}_{\cdot j} - \bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot})(y_{ij} - \bar{y}_{\cdot j})$$

Proof that the Cross-Product Term is Zero

Let's analyze the cross-product term: $$2 \sum_{j=1}^J \sum_{i=1}^n (\bar{y}_{\cdot j} - \bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot})(y_{ij} - \bar{y}_{\cdot j})$$ Since \((\bar{y}_{\cdot j} - \bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot})\) does not depend on the individual index \(i\), we can pull it out of the inner sum: $$2 \sum_{j=1}^J (\bar{y}_{\cdot j} - \bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot}) \left[ \sum_{i=1}^n (y_{ij} - \bar{y}_{\cdot j}) \right]$$ Now consider the term inside the brackets: $$\sum_{i=1}^n (y_{ij} - \bar{y}_{\cdot j}) = \sum_{i=1}^n y_{ij} - \sum_{i=1}^n \bar{y}_{\cdot j} = n \bar{y}_{\cdot j} - n \bar{y}_{\cdot j} = 0$$ Because the sum of deviations around any sample mean is always zero, the entire cross-product term is zero.

Therefore, the total sum of squares splits cleanly into two parts: $$\sum_{j=1}^J \sum_{i=1}^n (y_{ij} - \bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot})^2 = n \sum_{j=1}^J (\bar{y}_{\cdot j} - \bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot})^2 + \sum_{j=1}^J \sum_{i=1}^n (y_{ij} - \bar{y}_{\cdot j})^2$$ In standard notation, this is: $$SS_{\text{Total}} = SS_{\text{Between}} + SS_{\text{Within}}$$

3. Degrees of Freedom & Mean Squares

To turn these sums of squares into estimates of variance, we must divide them by their respective degrees of freedom (\(df\)):

  • Between-Group \(df\) (\(J-1\)): We are comparing \(J\) group means, but they are constrained to sum to the grand mean, removing 1 degree of freedom.
  • Within-Group \(df\) (\(J(n-1)\)): Inside each of the \(J\) groups, we have \(n\) observations. Estimating the group mean removes 1 degree of freedom per group, leaving \(n-1\) per group, summed over all \(J\) groups.

By dividing the Sum of Squares (\(SS\)) by the degrees of freedom (\(df\)), we obtain the Mean Squares (\(MS\)), which act as sample variance estimators: $$MS_{\text{Between}} = \frac{SS_{\text{Between}}}{J-1}, \quad MS_{\text{Within}} = \frac{SS_{\text{Within}}}{J(n-1)}$$

4. Derivation of Expected Mean Squares \(E(MS)\)

To see what these Mean Squares are actually estimating, we compute their expected values under our random-effects assumptions.

Deriving \(E(MS_{\text{Within}})\)

For any group \(j\), the observations \(y_{ij}\) given the group mean \(\theta_j\) are independent and identically distributed as \(N(\theta_j, \sigma^2)\). The sample variance within group \(j\) is: $$S_j^2 = \frac{1}{n-1} \sum_{i=1}^n (y_{ij} - \bar{y}_{\cdot j})^2$$ Since \(S_j^2\) is an unbiased estimator of the individual-level variance \(\sigma^2\), its expected value is: $$E[S_j^2] = \sigma^2$$ Summing over all groups \(j\), we find: $$E[SS_{\text{Within}}] = E\left[ \sum_{j=1}^J (n-1) S_j^2 \right] = \sum_{j=1}^J (n-1) \sigma^2 = J(n-1) \sigma^2$$ Dividing by the degrees of freedom \(J(n-1)\): $$E[MS_{\text{Within}}] = \sigma^2$$ This proves that \(MS_{\text{Within}}\) is an unbiased estimator of the individual-level measurement noise \(\sigma^2\).

Deriving \(E(MS_{\text{Between}})\)

Now let's examine the group means \(\bar{y}_{\cdot j}\). In our model: $$\bar{y}_{\cdot j} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (\mu + \alpha_j + \epsilon_{ij}) = \mu + \alpha_j + \bar{\epsilon}_{\cdot j}$$ where \(\bar{\epsilon}_{\cdot j} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \epsilon_{ij}\).

Since \(\alpha_j \sim N(0, \tau^2)\) and the average residual error is \(\bar{\epsilon}_{\cdot j} \sim N(0, \sigma^2/n)\), the group means are independent, normally distributed variables: $$\bar{y}_{\cdot j} \sim N\left(\mu, \tau^2 + \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)$$

The sample variance of these \(J\) independent group means is: $$S_{\text{means}}^2 = \frac{1}{J-1} \sum_{j=1}^J (\bar{y}_{\cdot j} - \bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot})^2$$ Since \(S_{\text{means}}^2\) is an unbiased estimator of the variance of \(\bar{y}_{\cdot j}\), its expected value must equal \(\text{var}(\bar{y}_{\cdot j})\): $$E[S_{\text{means}}^2] = \tau^2 + \frac{\sigma^2}{n}$$

Now we express the expected value of \(SS_{\text{Between}}\): $$E[SS_{\text{Between}}] = E\left[ n \sum_{j=1}^J (\bar{y}_{\cdot j} - \bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot})^2 \right] = n (J-1) E[S_{\text{means}}^2] = n(J-1) \left( \tau^2 + \frac{\sigma^2}{n} \right) = (J-1)(n\tau^2 + \sigma^2)$$

Dividing by the degrees of freedom \(J-1\) yields: $$E[MS_{\text{Between}}] = n\tau^2 + \sigma^2$$ This shows that the variation between group averages is driven by both the individual measurement noise (\(\sigma^2\)) and the true variation between group means (\(\tau^2\)), scaled by the group size \(n\).

5. The F-Statistic and Decision Rule

We want to test the null hypothesis: $$H_0: \tau^2 = 0 \quad \text{(all group means are identical)}$$ Under this null hypothesis, both expected mean squares simplify: $$E[MS_{\text{Within}}] = \sigma^2, \quad E[MS_{\text{Between}}] = \sigma^2$$

This means that under \(H_0\), both \(MS_{\text{Between}}\) and \(MS_{\text{Within}}\) are independent estimates of the exact same variance \(\sigma^2\). The ratio of two independent chi-square variables divided by their degrees of freedom follows an F-distribution: $$F = \frac{MS_{\text{Between}}}{MS_{\text{Within}}} \sim F_{J-1, \; J(n-1)}$$

If the true group-level variance \(\tau^2\) is greater than 0, then \(E[MS_{\text{Between}}] = n\tau^2 + \sigma^2 > \sigma^2\). In this case, the ratio \(F\) will tend to be significantly larger than 1.

The Classical Decision Rule

  • If \(F\) is statistically significant (large value): We reject the null hypothesis \(H_0\) and conclude that \(\tau^2 > 0\). Epistemologically, this means the groups are distinct enough to warrant separate group-specific estimates: \(\hat{\theta}_j = \bar{y}_{\cdot j}\) (no pooling).
  • If \(F\) is not statistically significant: We fail to reject \(H_0\). Classical statistics concludes that we do not have enough evidence to claim the groups are different, leading to the decision to use a single pooled estimate: \(\hat{\theta}_j = \bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot}\) for all \(j\) (complete pooling).

As Andrew Gelman points out in Section 5.4, this binary "all-or-nothing" choice is a major limitation of classical inference. Rather than forcing a choice between the extremes of complete pooling and separate estimates, the Bayesian hierarchical approach uses the data to estimate the actual value of \(\tau\), creating a continuous compromise (shrinkage) based on our real uncertainty.
