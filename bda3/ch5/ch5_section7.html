5.7 Weakly Informative Priors for Variance Parameters

Evaluating prior distributions for the group-level scale parameter $\tau$, exploring how noninformative priors affect posterior inferences for small $J$, and analyzing the advantages of the half-Cauchy prior.

A key element in the analyses of conjugate and normal hierarchical models is the prior distribution for the scale parameter, $\tau$. We have previously used the uniform prior distribution, but various other noninformative prior distributions have been suggested in the Bayesian literature. It turns out that the choice of "noninformative" prior distribution can have a big effect on inferences, especially for problems where the number of groups $J$ is small or the group-level variation $\tau$ is small.

We discuss the options here in the context of the normal model, but the principles apply to inferences for group-level variances more generally.

Concepts Relating to the Choice of Prior Distribution

Improper Limit of a Prior Distribution

Improper prior densities can, but do not necessarily, lead to proper posterior distributions. To avoid confusion, it is useful to define improper distributions as particular limits of proper distributions. For the group-level variance parameter, two commonly considered improper densities are:

  • $\text{Uniform}(0, A)$ on $\tau$, as $A \to \infty$
  • $\text{Inverse-gamma}(\epsilon, \epsilon)$ on $\tau^2$, as $\epsilon \to 0$

The $\text{Uniform}(0, A)$ model yields a limiting proper posterior density as $A \to \infty$, as long as the number of groups $J$ is at least 3. Thus, for a finite but sufficiently large $A$, inferences are not sensitive to the choice of $A$. In contrast, the $\text{Inverse-gamma}(\epsilon, \epsilon)$ model does not have any proper limiting posterior distribution. As a result, posterior inferences are sensitive to $\epsilon$—it cannot simply be comfortably set to a low value such as $0.001$.

Calibration

Posterior inferences can be evaluated using the concept of calibration of the posterior mean, which is the Bayesian analogue to the classical notion of bias. For any parameter $\theta$, if we label the posterior mean as $\hat{\theta} = E(\theta \mid y)$, we can define the miscalibration of the posterior mean as $E(\theta \mid \hat{\theta}) - \hat{\theta}$.

If the prior distribution is true—that is, if the data are constructed by first drawing $\theta$ from $p(\theta)$, then drawing $y$ from $p(y \mid \theta)$—then the posterior mean is automatically calibrated; that is, the miscalibration is $0$ for all values of $\hat{\theta}$.

Note the distinction: In classical bias analysis, we condition on the true $\theta$ and look at the distribution of the data-based estimate, $\hat{\theta}$. In a Bayesian calibration analysis, we condition on the data $y$ (and thus also on the estimate, $\hat{\theta}$) and look at the distribution of parameters $\theta$ that could have produced these data.

When considering improper models, the theory must be expanded, since it is impossible for $\theta$ to be drawn from an unnormalized density. To evaluate calibration in this context, it is necessary to posit a "true prior distribution" from which $\theta$ is drawn, along with the "inferential prior distribution" that is used in the Bayesian inference.

For the hierarchical model for the 8 schools, we can consider the improper uniform density on $\tau$ as a limit of uniform prior densities on the range $(0, A)$, with $A \to \infty$. For any finite value of $A$, we can then see that the improper uniform density leads to inferences with a positive miscalibration—that is, overestimates (on average) of $\tau$.

Classes of Noninformative and Weakly Informative Priors

We view any noninformative or weakly informative prior distribution as inherently provisional—after the model has been fit, one should look at the posterior distribution and see if it makes sense. If the posterior distribution does not make sense, this implies that additional prior knowledge is available that has not been included in the model, and that contradicts the assumptions of the prior distribution that has been used. It is then appropriate to go back and alter the prior distribution to be more consistent with this external knowledge.

1. Uniform Prior Distributions

We must be explicit about the scale on which the uniform distribution is defined:

  • Uniform on $\log\tau$: Equivalent to $p(\tau) \propto \tau^{-1}$. This results in an improper posterior distribution because the marginal likelihood $p(y \mid \tau)$ approaches a finite nonzero value as $\tau \to 0$. The posterior will have infinite mass integrating to $\log\tau \to -\infty$. Because the data can never rule out a group-level variance of zero, putting infinite mass near zero causes the model to fail.
  • Uniform on $\tau$: Equivalent to $p(\tau^2) \propto \tau^{-1}$. It has a finite integral near $\tau = 0$ and avoids the log-scale issues. However, it has a positive miscalibration toward larger values. With $J=1$ or $2$ groups, it results in an improper posterior density, effectively concluding $\tau = \infty$ (doing no pooling). For small $J$ (like $4$ or $5$), the heavy right tail of the posterior can lead to overestimates of $\tau$ and thus suboptimal pooling.
  • Uniform on $\tau^2$: Equivalent to $p(\tau) \propto \tau$. We do not recommend this as its miscalibration toward higher values is even worse, and it requires $J \ge 4$ groups for a proper posterior distribution.

2. Inverse-Gamma prior distributions

The parameter $\tau$ does not have any simple conjugate prior because its marginal likelihood depends in a complex way on the data. However, the inverse-gamma family is conditionally conjugate given the other parameters ($\mu, \theta$): if $\tau^2$ has an inverse-gamma prior, its conditional posterior $p(\tau^2 \mid \theta, \mu, y)$ is also inverse-gamma.

The $\text{Inverse-gamma}(\epsilon, \epsilon)$ prior distribution is an attempt at noninformativeness (with $\epsilon = 0.001$, say). However, in the limit $\epsilon \to 0$ it yields an improper posterior density. For datasets where low values of $\tau$ are plausible, inferences become extremely sensitive to the choice of $\epsilon$, meaning the prior is highly informative in a way that distorts the posterior.

3. Half-Cauchy Prior Distributions

The half-$t$ family (constrained to be positive since scale $\tau \ge 0$) offers an excellent weakly informative family. A key member is the half-Cauchy prior with scale parameter $A$: $$p(\tau) \propto \frac{1}{1 + (\tau/A)^2}, \quad \tau \ge 0$$

It has a broad, flat peak at zero (allowing complete pooling if supported by data) and a heavy tail. In the limit $A \to \infty$, it becomes the uniform prior on $\tau$. Large but finite values of $A$ represent weakly informative priors: they have a gentle slope in the tail and let the data dominate if the likelihood is strong, but successfully regularize the posterior if the number of groups is small.

As shown in the charts:

  • The Uniform prior on $\tau$ (left) allows the posterior to reflect the data, support values below 20, and drop off in the tail. It does not constrain the posterior.
  • The Inverse-gamma(1, 1) model (middle) heavily restricts the posterior, squeezing it to a narrow zone where the prior is concentrated. The posterior is dominated by the prior, not the data.
  • The Inverse-gamma(0.001, 0.001) model (right) spikes heavily near zero. This is because the likelihood remains high near zero, and the massive prior spike pulls the posterior into a degenerate spike at zero.

Why the Inverse-Gamma Prior is a Trap

If you write down $\text{Inverse-gamma}(0.001, 0.001)$, it sounds incredibly safe. "Look," you say, "the parameters are $0.001$. That is practically zero. It must be completely flat and noninformative!"

1. The Illusion of Flatness

It is flat on the log scale, but not on the standard scale. If we plot it on the standard deviation scale $\tau$, this prior has a massive, towering spike as $\tau \to 0$. It is like a gravity well right next to zero. If your data doesn't have a massive force pushing it away from zero (which happens when $J$ is small), the posterior gets sucked into this gravity well and collapses at $\tau = 0$.

2. Sensitivity to arbitrary thresholds

If you change the prior from $\text{Inverse-gamma}(0.001, 0.001)$ to $\text{Inverse-gamma}(0.00001, 0.00001)$, your results change drastically. If your "noninformative" prior changes your final answer just because you added a few zeros to an arbitrary constant, it is not noninformative—it is highly influential, and highly dangerous.

Weakly Informative Prior for the 3-Schools Problem (Figure 5.10)

While the uniform prior on $\tau$ worked well with 8 schools, it fails if the number of groups $J$ is smaller. With only 3 schools ($J=3$), the data supply almost no information about the group-level variance.

Figure 5.10: Posterior simulations of $\tau$ in the 3-schools problem

(a) Uniform(0, ∞) Prior
(b) Half-Cauchy(25) Prior

As displayed in Figure 5.10:

  • The Uniform prior (left) produces a posterior with an extremely heavy right tail. It contains values of $\tau > 100$ that are clinically and educationally impossible. This leads to severe underpooling, meaning we treat the three schools as completely separate, losing the benefits of hierarchical modeling.
  • The Half-Cauchy prior with scale $A=25$ (right) constrains the posterior, gracefully cutting off the unrealistic upper tail while remaining flat and non-restrictive in the high-likelihood region ($\tau < 50$). It regularizes the model without distorting the data's message.
