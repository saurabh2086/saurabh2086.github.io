First-Principles Derivation

Exchangeable Prior Distributions & de Finetti's Limit

Suppose we know a priori that $2J$ parameters are split into two equal groups, half drawn from $N(1, 1)$ and the other half from $N(-1, 1)$. We do not know which parameter came from which group. This problem forces us to mathematically dissect exchangeability, compute the negative covariance generated by finite global constraints, and examine why this physical restriction disappears in the infinite limit.

Exercise Description

Suppose it is known a priori that the $2J$ parameters $\theta_1, \dots, \theta_{2J}$ are clustered into two groups, with exactly half being drawn from a $N(1, 1)$ distribution and the other half from a $N(-1, 1)$ distribution, but we have not observed which parameters come from which distribution.

  1. Are $\theta_1, \dots, \theta_{2J}$ exchangeable under this prior distribution?
  2. Show that this distribution cannot be written as a mixture of independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) components.
  3. Why can we not simply take the limit as $J \to \infty$ and get a counterexample to de Finetti's theorem?

1. Part A: Permutation Invariance (Exchangeability)

To show that the parameters are exchangeable, we must prove that the joint prior density $p(\theta_1, \dots, \theta_{2J})$ is invariant under any permutation of its indices.

Let $z = (z_1, \dots, z_{2J}) \in \{0, 1\}^{2J}$ be the vector of indicators, where $z_j = 1$ indicates $\theta_j$ is drawn from $N(1, 1)$, and $z_j = 0$ indicates $\theta_j$ is drawn from $N(-1, 1)$. The prior constraint dictates that exactly half of the parameters belong to each cluster. The set of all valid configurations is:

$$Z = \left\{ z \in \{0, 1\}^{2J} : \sum_{j=1}^{2J} z_j = J \right\}$$

Since we have no prior information about which parameter belongs to which cluster, the distribution over these configurations is uniform:

$$p(z) = \frac{1}{\binom{2J}{J}} \quad \text{for } z \in Z$$

Given $z$, the parameters are conditionally independent:

$$p(\theta \mid z) = \prod_{j=1}^{2J} p(\theta_j \mid z_j) = \prod_{j=1}^{2J} N(\theta_j \mid \mu(z_j), 1)$$

where $\mu(1) = 1$ and $\mu(0) = -1$. The marginal joint density is the mixture over all configurations:

$$p(\theta) = \frac{1}{\binom{2J}{J}} \sum_{z \in Z} \prod_{j=1}^{2J} N(\theta_j \mid \mu(z_j), 1)$$

Now apply a permutation $\pi$ to the parameter indices. The permuted density is:

$$p(\theta_{\pi(1)}, \dots, \theta_{\pi(2J)}) = \frac{1}{\binom{2J}{J}} \sum_{z \in Z} \prod_{j=1}^{2J} N(\theta_{\pi(j)} \mid \mu(z_j), 1)$$

Re-index the product terms by setting $i = \pi(j)$ (which implies $j = \pi^{-1}(i)$):

$$p(\theta_{\pi(1)}, \dots, \theta_{\pi(2J)}) = \frac{1}{\binom{2J}{J}} \sum_{z \in Z} \prod_{i=1}^{2J} N(\theta_i \mid \mu(z_{\pi^{-1}(i)}), 1)$$

Because $\pi$ is a bijection, the permuted indicator vector $z_{\pi^{-1}}$ has exactly the same number of ones as $z$. Thus, $z \in Z \iff z_{\pi^{-1}} \in Z$. The mapping $z \mapsto z_{\pi^{-1}}$ merely permutes the summation terms over the finite set $Z$. The total sum is unchanged:

$$p(\theta_{\pi(1)}, \dots, \theta_{\pi(2J)}) = \frac{1}{\binom{2J}{J}} \sum_{z' \in Z} \prod_{i=1}^{2J} N(\theta_i \mid \mu(z'_i), 1) = p(\theta)$$
Conclusion for Part A

The joint prior density is completely symmetric under index shuffling. Therefore, $\theta_1, \dots, \theta_{2J}$ are exchangeable.

2. Part B: Non-i.i.d. Mixture Representation

A prior distribution is a mixture of i.i.d. components if and only if it can be represented as:

$$p(\theta) = \int \prod_{j=1}^{2J} p(\theta_j \mid \phi) p(\phi) d\phi$$

Let $\mu(\phi) = E[\theta_j \mid \phi]$ be the conditional expectation. For any two distinct parameters $\theta_a$ and $\theta_b$ ($a \neq b$), conditional independence yields:

$$E[\theta_a \theta_b] = E[E[\theta_a \theta_b \mid \phi]] = E[E[\theta_a \mid \phi] E[\theta_b \mid \phi]] = E[\mu(\phi)^2]$$

And the product of their expectations is:

$$E[\theta_a] E[\theta_b] = (E[E[\theta_a \mid \phi]])^2 = (E[\mu(\phi)])^2$$

The covariance between any two distinct variables in an i.i.d. mixture is therefore:

$$\text{cov}(\theta_a, \theta_b) = E[\mu(\phi)^2] - (E[\mu(\phi)])^2 = \text{var}(\mu(\phi)) \ge 0$$

Because a variance is always non-negative, **any mixture of i.i.d. components must have a non-negative covariance between its variables.** Let's compute the covariance of our prior.

Derivation of Prior Covariance

Let $S = \sum_{i=1}^{2J} \theta_i$ be the sum of all variables. Expanding the variance of the sum:

$$\text{var}(S) = \sum_{i=1}^{2J} \text{var}(\theta_i) + \sum_{i \neq j} \text{cov}(\theta_i, \theta_j)$$ $$\text{var}(S) = 2J \text{var}(\theta_i) + 2J(2J - 1) c$$

where $c = \text{cov}(\theta_i, \theta_j)$ is the shared covariance by exchangeability.

We compute $\text{var}(S)$ using the law of total variance, conditioning on the indicator configuration $z$:

$$\text{var}(S) = E[\text{var}(S \mid z)] + \text{var}(E[S \mid z])$$

* **Conditional Variance**: Given $z$, $\theta_i$ are independent with variance $1$. Thus, $\text{var}(S \mid z) = \sum \text{var}(\theta_i \mid z_i) = 2J \cdot 1 = 2J$. This is a constant, so $E[\text{var}(S \mid z)] = 2J$.
* **Conditional Expectation**: Given $z$, exactly $J$ variables have mean $1$ and $J$ variables have mean $-1$. Thus, $E[S \mid z] = J(1) + J(-1) = 0$. This is a constant, so $\text{var}(E[S \mid z]) = \text{var}(0) = 0$.

Therefore, the total variance of the sum is:

$$\text{var}(S) = 2J$$

Next, we compute the marginal variance $\text{var}(\theta_i)$ by conditioning on $z_i$:

$$\text{var}(\theta_i) = E[\text{var}(\theta_i \mid z_i)] + \text{var}(E[\theta_i \mid z_i])$$

* **Conditional Variance**: $\text{var}(\theta_i \mid z_i) = 1$, so $E[\text{var}(\theta_i \mid z_i)] = 1$.
* **Conditional Expectation**: $E[\theta_i \mid z_i]$ is $+1$ with probability $0.5$ and $-1$ with probability $0.5$. Its expected value is $0$, and its variance is $\text{var}(E[\theta_i \mid z_i]) = 0.5(1)^2 + 0.5(-1)^2 = 1$.

This gives the marginal variance:

$$\text{var}(\theta_i) = 1 + 1 = 2$$

Now substitute $\text{var}(S) = 2J$ and $\text{var}(\theta_i) = 2$ back into the sum expansion:

$$2J = 2J(2) + 2J(2J-1) c$$ $$2J = 4J + 2J(2J-1) c$$ $$-2J = 2J(2J-1) c$$ $$c = -\frac{1}{2J - 1}$$
Conclusion for Part B

For all $J \ge 1$, the covariance between distinct variables is strictly negative:

$$\text{cov}(\theta_i, \theta_j) = -\frac{1}{2J-1} < 0$$

Since a mixture of i.i.d. components requires $\text{cov}(\theta_i, \theta_j) \ge 0$, this distribution cannot be written as a mixture of i.i.d. components.

3. Part C: Resolution of the de Finetti Limit

De Finetti's theorem states that any infinite exchangeable sequence can be written as a mixture of i.i.d. components. Why does this finite counterexample not contradict the theorem when we take the limit $J \to \infty$?

The key is **infinite extendability**. De Finetti's theorem applies to a single, infinite exchangeable sequence of variables.

For a fixed $J$, the joint distribution is defined on a finite sequence of length $2J$. We cannot extend this specific joint distribution to an infinite sequence. For example, if we have a sequence of size $2J$ and attempt to add two more variables to make it size $2J+2$, the constraint "exactly half are $+1$" forces us to change the joint distribution of the first $2J$ parameters (their covariance changes from $-1/(2J-1)$ to $-1/(2J+1)$).

As we take the limit $J \to \infty$, the negative covariance vanishes:

$$\lim_{J \to \infty} \text{cov}(\theta_i, \theta_j) = \lim_{J \to \infty} -\frac{1}{2J-1} = 0$$

In this infinite limit, the variables become conditionally independent. The joint distribution of the infinite sequence converges to a mixture of i.i.d. components:

$$p(\theta) = \prod_{j=1}^{\infty} \left( 0.5 N(\theta_j \mid 1, 1) + 0.5 N(\theta_j \mid -1, 1) \right)$$
Conclusion for Part C

The finite-length prior cannot be extended to an infinite sequence without changing the underlying distribution. In the limit where the sequence does become infinite, the negative correlation pressure drops to $0$, and we recover a true i.i.d. mixture. There is no contradiction.
