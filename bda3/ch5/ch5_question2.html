Exchangeability with Unknown Parameters

Exploring how our learning process inherently links observations together, even when the physical sampling process replaces them.

The Core Principle: Marginal vs. Conditional

The Learning Effect

When the underlying parameters (e.g., the proportion of black balls $\theta$) are unknown, observing the first piece of data $y_1$ teaches us something about $\theta$. This newly acquired knowledge inherently changes our predictions for $y_2$.

$$p(y_2 | y_1) \neq p(y_2)$$

Even if the physical process is entirely isolated (like drawing with replacement), our epistemological state links the two events. They are conditionally independent given $\theta$, but marginally dependent because $\theta$ is unknown.

Analyzing the Scenarios

A

With Replacement (Unknown Composition)

"A box has $n$ black and white balls but we do not know how many of each color. We pick a ball $y_1$ at random, put it back, and pick another ball $y_2$ at random."

(i) Are they Exchangeable?

Yes. The order in which we learn about the box doesn't matter. The joint distribution $p(y_1, y_2)$ is symmetric because it is derived by integrating over all possible unknown compositions. Therefore, $p(y_1, y_2) = p(y_2, y_1)$.

(ii) Are they Independent?

No. Because we don't know the composition, $y_1$ is informative. If $y_1$ is Black, we increase our estimate of the proportion of black balls $\theta$. Mathematically, using the law of total covariance: $$\mathrm{Cov}(y_1, y_2) = E[\mathrm{Cov}(y_1, y_2 \mid \theta)] + \mathrm{Var}(E[y_1 \mid \theta]) = 0 + \mathrm{Var}(\theta) = \mathrm{Var}(\theta)$$ Since the parameter is unknown, $\mathrm{Var}(\theta) > 0$, giving a positive covariance and indicating dependency.

(iii) Can we Act Independent?

No. Acting as if they are independent implies that observing $y_1$ teaches us absolutely nothing about the box. If we did that, we would never be able to learn or update our posterior distribution.

B

Without Replacement (Unknown Composition)

"A box has $n$ black and white balls but we do not know how many of each color. We pick a ball $y_1$ at random, we do not put it back, then we pick ball $y_2$."

(i) Are they Exchangeable?

Yes. Symmetry holds. Before any balls are drawn, any specific sequence (like Black, then White) is just as likely as its permutation (White, then Black). The lack of knowledge preserves the symmetry of our ignorance.

(ii) Are they Independent?

No. They are dependent for two reasons: the physical depletion of the box, and the epistemic learning about $\theta$. Mathematically: $$\mathrm{Cov}(y_1, y_2) = \mathrm{Var}(\theta) - \frac{E[\theta(1-\theta)]}{N-1}$$ Here we see the positive learning effect ($\mathrm{Var}(\theta)$) competing with the negative physical depletion effect ($-\frac{E[\theta(1-\theta)]}{N-1}$). The non-zero covariance confirms dependency.

(iii) Can we Act Independent?

Absolutely Not. The observations are highly intertwined both epistemologically (learning) and physically (depletion).

C

Without Replacement (Unknown Vast Population)

"Same as (b) but we know that there are many balls of each color in the box."

(i) Are they Exchangeable?

Yes. Symmetry holds universally across all these variations.

(ii) Are they Independent?

No. Because the population is vast, the physical effect of removing a single ball is negligible ($-\frac{E[\theta(1-\theta)]}{N-1} \to 0$). However, the learning effect remains! Mathematically: $$\lim_{N\to\infty} \mathrm{Cov}(y_1, y_2) = \mathrm{Var}(\theta) > 0$$ Because the covariance converges to the positive variance $\mathrm{Var}(\theta)$, the draws remain dependent.

(iii) Can we Act Independent?

No. This is the crucial distinction from Question 1. Even though the population is infinite, we cannot treat the draws as independent because we must use them to learn the underlying parameter. If we acted independently, Bayesian inference would be impossible.

The "Why" Behind the Math

This question contrasts perfectly with Question 1 to highlight the difference between physical independence and statistical independence in a Bayesian framework.

In frequentist statistics, if you draw with replacement, the events are considered independent because the physical mechanism isolates them. But in Bayesian statistics, if the underlying parameter is unknown, your observations are never marginally independent. The very act of observing data is what allows you to learn. If observations were truly independent, past data would tell you absolutely nothing about future data!

Key Derivation: One Formula for Every Case

A single identity — the law of total covariance — produces the covariance for all six scenarios across Questions 1 and 2. Let $y_i = 1$ for black, $0$ for white, and let $\theta$ be the black fraction. Conditioning on $\theta$ splits the dependence into two named pieces:

$$\mathrm{Cov}(y_1, y_2) = \underbrace{E\big[\mathrm{Cov}(y_1,y_2 \mid \theta)\big]}_{\text{physical (depletion)}} \;+\; \underbrace{\mathrm{Cov}\big(E[y_1\mid\theta],\, E[y_2\mid\theta]\big)}_{\text{learning}} $$ $$= \; E\big[\mathrm{Cov}(y_1,y_2 \mid \theta)\big] \;+\; \mathrm{Var}(\theta)$$

The second term is $\mathrm{Var}(\theta)$ because $E[y_i \mid \theta] = \theta$, so $\mathrm{Cov}(\theta,\theta) = \mathrm{Var}(\theta)$ — this is the learning effect. The first term is the physical effect, which depends on the draw mechanism:

  • With replacement: draws are conditionally independent, so $\mathrm{Cov}(y_1,y_2\mid\theta) = 0$.
  • Without replacement (box of $N$ balls): $\mathrm{Cov}(y_1,y_2\mid\theta) = -\dfrac{\theta(1-\theta)}{N-1}$, so the term is $-\dfrac{E[\theta(1-\theta)]}{N-1}$.

The case $\theta$ unknown (Question 2)

Detailed expansion of the with-replacement case (2a), where the physical term is zero:

$$\begin{aligned} \mathrm{Cov}(y_1, y_2) &= E[y_1 y_2] - E[y_1]\,E[y_2] \\[4pt] &= E\big[\,E[y_1\mid\theta]\,E[y_2\mid\theta]\,\big] - \big(E[\theta]\big)^2 \\[4pt] &= E[\theta^2] - \big(E[\theta]\big)^2 = \mathrm{Var}(\theta) \end{aligned}$$
  • (a) With replacement: $\mathrm{Cov} = \mathrm{Var}(\theta)$ — dependent (learning only).
  • (b) Without replacement, box of $N$: $\mathrm{Cov} = \mathrm{Var}(\theta) - \dfrac{E[\theta(1-\theta)]}{N-1}$ — both effects (learning $+$ depletion).
  • (c) Without replacement, many balls ($N\to\infty$): depletion $\to 0$, so $\mathrm{Cov} \to \mathrm{Var}(\theta)$ — dependent, learning survives.

The case $\theta$ known (Question 1)

When $\theta$ is known it is a constant, so $\mathrm{Var}(\theta) = 0$ and only the physical term survives:

  • (a) With replacement: $\mathrm{Cov} = 0$ — strictly independent.
  • (b) Without replacement, $N=2$, $\theta=\tfrac12$: $\mathrm{Cov} = -\dfrac{1}{4}$ — strongly dependent.
  • (c) Without replacement, $N$ huge: $\mathrm{Cov} = -\dfrac{\theta(1-\theta)}{N-1} \to 0$ — act independent.

The whole pair of problems collapses to one line: $\mathrm{Cov} = \mathrm{Var}(\theta) - \frac{E[\theta(1-\theta)]}{N-1}$ (with replacement drops the second term). Population size $N$ only shrinks the physical term; only knowing $\theta$ removes the learning term $\mathrm{Var}(\theta)$. That single distinction is why 1(c) is independent and 2(c) is not. The deeper reason it all coheres is de Finetti's theorem: an exchangeable sequence is a mixture of i.i.d. sequences, and mixing over the unknown $\theta$ is exactly what couples the observations and lets data teach us.
