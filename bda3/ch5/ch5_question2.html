Exchangeability with Unknown Parameters

Exploring how our learning process inherently links observations together, even when the physical sampling process replaces them.

The Core Principle: Marginal vs. Conditional

The Learning Effect

When the underlying parameters (e.g., the proportion of black balls $\theta$) are unknown, observing the first piece of data $y_1$ teaches us something about $\theta$. This newly acquired knowledge inherently changes our predictions for $y_2$.

$$p(y_2 | y_1) \neq p(y_2)$$

Even if the physical process is entirely isolated (like drawing with replacement), our epistemological state links the two events. They are conditionally independent given $\theta$, but marginally dependent because $\theta$ is unknown.

Analyzing the Scenarios

A

With Replacement (Unknown Composition)

"A box has $n$ black and white balls but we do not know how many of each color. We pick a ball $y_1$ at random, put it back, and pick another ball $y_2$ at random."

(i) Are they Exchangeable?

Yes. The order in which we learn about the box doesn't matter. The joint distribution $p(y_1, y_2)$ is symmetric because it is derived by integrating over all possible unknown compositions. Therefore, $p(y_1, y_2) = p(y_2, y_1)$.

(ii) Are they Independent?

No. Because we don't know the composition, $y_1$ is informative. If $y_1$ is Black, we increase our estimate of the number of black balls in the box. This makes us more likely to predict that $y_2$ will also be Black. Our beliefs are linked.

(iii) Can we Act Independent?

No. Acting as if they are independent implies that observing $y_1$ teaches us absolutely nothing about the box. If we did that, we would never be able to learn or update our posterior distribution.

B

Without Replacement (Unknown Composition)

"A box has $n$ black and white balls but we do not know how many of each color. We pick a ball $y_1$ at random, we do not put it back, then we pick ball $y_2$."

(i) Are they Exchangeable?

Yes. Symmetry holds. Before any balls are drawn, any specific sequence (like Black, then White) is just as likely as its permutation (White, then Black). The lack of knowledge preserves the symmetry of our ignorance.

(ii) Are they Independent?

No. They are dependent for two reasons now. First, the learning effect: $y_1$ gives us clues about the initial unknown proportion. Second, the physical effect: $y_1$ literally alters the composition of the box for the next draw.

(iii) Can we Act Independent?

Absolutely Not. The observations are highly intertwined both epistemologically (learning) and physically (depletion).

C

Without Replacement (Unknown Vast Population)

"Same as (b) but we know that there are many balls of each color in the box."

(i) Are they Exchangeable?

Yes. Symmetry holds universally across all these variations.

(ii) Are they Independent?

No. Because the population is vast, the physical effect of removing a single ball is negligible (as seen in Question 1c). However, the learning effect remains! Because the true proportion is unknown, drawing $y_1$ still shifts our belief about that underlying proportion.

(iii) Can we Act Independent?

No. This is the crucial distinction from Question 1. Even though the population is infinite, we cannot treat the draws as independent because we must use them to learn the underlying parameter. If we acted independently, Bayesian inference would be impossible.

The "Why" Behind the Math

This question contrasts perfectly with Question 1 to highlight the difference between physical independence and statistical independence in a Bayesian framework.

In frequentist statistics, if you draw with replacement, the events are considered independent because the physical mechanism isolates them. But in Bayesian statistics, if the underlying parameter is unknown, your observations are never marginally independent. The very act of observing data is what allows you to learn. If observations were truly independent, past data would tell you absolutely nothing about future data!
