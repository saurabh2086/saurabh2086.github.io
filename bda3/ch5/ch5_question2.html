Chapter 5 • Question 2
Let's Figure It Out

Now We Don't Know What's in the Box

Same box, same two draws — except this time nobody told us how many balls are black. That one change does something marvelous. The draws become tied together not because of any physics, but purely because of our own ignorance. We'll pin that down to a single number, $\mathrm{Cov}(y_1,y_2)=\mathrm{Var}(\theta)$, and then watch de Finetti's theorem turn "I'll treat these as exchangeable" into a full-blown hierarchical model, whether you asked for one or not.

1. The One New Ingredient

Back in Question 1 the fraction $\theta$ was a number we knew. Now we don't know it. So what do we do? We admit the ignorance and treat $\theta$ itself as a random thing, with some distribution $p(\theta)$ spread over $[0,1]$ describing what we believe before drawing. And here's the nice part: once you fix a value of $\theta$, you're right back in Question 1.

So the recipe for the whole experiment has two steps:

$$\theta \sim p(\theta), \qquad y_i \mid \theta \;\sim\; \text{(draw from a box whose black fraction is } \theta).$$

Write $E[\theta]$ for the average we expect and $\mathrm{Var}(\theta)$ for how unsure we are. Everything — literally the whole question of whether the draws are independent — is going to come down to that one number, $\mathrm{Var}(\theta)$, and whether it's zero.

Here's the idea to hold in your head before we do any algebra. Even though the two draws are independent once you know $\theta$, they are tangled up when you don't — because the first ball is a little clue about $\theta$, and $\theta$ is what governs the second ball. Knowledge leaks from $y_1$ to $y_2$ through $\theta$. Let's make that exact.

2. Two Little Tools (and We'll Prove Them)

We need two facts. I don't want you to take them on faith, so let's actually derive them — they're both short.

Tool 1 — the average of an average

The claim is $\;E[X] = E\big[\,E[X\mid\theta]\,\big].$ All this says is: to get the overall average, first average for each fixed $\theta$, then average those over $\theta$. Two stages of bookkeeping:

$$E[X] = \sum_\theta \Big(\textstyle\sum_x x\, p(x\mid\theta)\Big) p(\theta) = \sum_\theta E[X\mid\theta]\,p(\theta) = E\big[E[X\mid\theta]\big].$$
Tool 2 — splitting the covariance in two

This is the workhorse. It says the covariance splits into a piece from inside each $\theta$ and a piece from the spread between different $\theta$'s:

$$\mathrm{Cov}(X,Y) = \underbrace{E\big[\mathrm{Cov}(X,Y\mid\theta)\big]}_{\text{the physics}} + \underbrace{\mathrm{Cov}\big(E[X\mid\theta],\,E[Y\mid\theta]\big)}_{\text{the learning}}.$$

Let's prove it. Start from the definition $\mathrm{Cov}(X,Y)=E[XY]-E[X]E[Y]$, and crack open $E[XY]$ by conditioning on $\theta$:

$$\begin{aligned} E[XY] &= E\big[E[XY\mid\theta]\big] = E\big[\,\mathrm{Cov}(X,Y\mid\theta) + E[X\mid\theta]\,E[Y\mid\theta]\,\big]\\[4pt] &= E\big[\mathrm{Cov}(X,Y\mid\theta)\big] + E\big[E[X\mid\theta]\,E[Y\mid\theta]\big]. \end{aligned}$$

Then subtract $E[X]E[Y] = E\big[E[X\mid\theta]\big]\,E\big[E[Y\mid\theta]\big]$. The leftover two terms are exactly the covariance of the conditional means:

$$\mathrm{Cov}(X,Y) = E\big[\mathrm{Cov}(X,Y\mid\theta)\big] + \Big(E\big[E[X\mid\theta]E[Y\mid\theta]\big] - E\big[E[X\mid\theta]\big]E\big[E[Y\mid\theta]\big]\Big). \qquad\blacksquare$$

I gave the two pieces nicknames on purpose. The first is the physics — the within-each-box sampling effect, averaged out. The second is the learning — how much the average draw wiggles as $\theta$ wiggles. Watch how cleanly these map onto our two scenarios.

A

Put It Back, but $\theta$ Is a Mystery

Once $\theta$ is fixed, putting the ball back means the two draws are independent coins, so $E[y_i\mid\theta]=\theta$. The one step that really deserves proof — and it's the engine of this whole result — is the product term $E[y_1 y_2\mid\theta]$. Let's not wave at it; let's compute it.

Here's the trick that makes it easy: since each $y_i$ is either $0$ or $1$, the product $y_1 y_2$ is also just $0$ or $1$, and it equals $1$ only when both balls come out black. So its average is simply the probability that both are black. Write the expectation out as a sum over the four possible outcomes and watch three of them die:

$$\begin{aligned} E[y_1 y_2 \mid \theta] &= \sum_{a\in\{0,1\}}\sum_{b\in\{0,1\}} a\,b\;P(y_1=a,\,y_2=b \mid \theta)\\[4pt] &= 1\cdot P(y_1=1,\,y_2=1\mid\theta) \qquad(\text{only the }a=b=1\text{ term survives})\\[4pt] &= P(y_1=1\mid\theta)\,P(y_2=1\mid\theta) \qquad(\text{independent given }\theta)\\[4pt] &= \theta\cdot\theta = \theta^2. \end{aligned}$$

That's the key fact: $E[y_1 y_2 \mid \theta] = \theta^2$. Now the rest is just turning the crank with our two tools:

$$\begin{aligned} E[y_1 y_2] &= E\big[E[y_1 y_2\mid\theta]\big] = E[\theta^2],\\[4pt] E[y_i] &= E\big[E[y_i\mid\theta]\big] = E[\theta],\\[6pt] \mathrm{Cov}(y_1,y_2) &= E[\theta^2] - \big(E[\theta]\big)^2 = \boxed{\,\mathrm{Var}(\theta)\,}. \end{aligned}$$

(The "physics" term is now visibly zero: $\mathrm{Cov}(y_1,y_2\mid\theta) = E[y_1y_2\mid\theta] - E[y_1\mid\theta]E[y_2\mid\theta] = \theta^2 - \theta^2 = 0$, exactly as putting the ball back demands.)

Stop and admire that. There is no physics here at all — you put the ball back, the box is untouched — and yet the covariance isn't zero. It's the variance of your own uncertainty.

(i) Exchangeable? Yes — the joint $p(y_1,y_2)=\int \theta^{y_1+y_2}(1-\theta)^{2-y_1-y_2}p(\theta)\,d\theta$ only sees the sum $y_1+y_2$.

(ii) Independent? Only if $\mathrm{Var}(\theta)=0$, i.e. only if you actually knew $\theta$. Since you don't, $\mathrm{Cov}=\mathrm{Var}(\theta)>0$ — a positive link conjured out of pure ignorance.

(iii) Pretend independent? No — to do that is to freeze $\theta$ at a guess and slam the door on learning anything from the data.

B

Keep It Out, and $\theta$ Is a Mystery

Now both effects are in play at once. For a fixed $\theta$ we're exactly in Question 1(b), so the physics term is $\mathrm{Cov}(y_1,y_2\mid\theta)=-\dfrac{\theta(1-\theta)}{N-1}$ and the means are still $\theta$. Feed those into Tool 2:

$$\begin{aligned} \mathrm{Cov}(y_1,y_2) &= E\!\left[-\frac{\theta(1-\theta)}{N-1}\right] + \mathrm{Cov}(\theta,\theta)\\[6pt] &= \mathrm{Var}(\theta) \;-\; \frac{E[\theta(1-\theta)]}{N-1}. \end{aligned}$$

(i) Exchangeable? Yes — it's symmetric for each $\theta$, and averaging over $p(\theta)$ keeps it symmetric.

(ii)–(iii) Independent? No — and now you can literally see the tug-of-war: a positive learning pull, $\mathrm{Var}(\theta)$, fighting a negative depletion push. In a small box the depletion can win and drag the whole thing negative. Doesn't matter which wins — they're linked, so you can't treat them as independent.

C

Keep It Out, Huge Box, $\theta$ Still a Mystery

Let the box get enormous. The depletion push dies away, but the learning pull doesn't budge:

$$\mathrm{Cov}(y_1,y_2) = \mathrm{Var}(\theta) - \frac{E[\theta(1-\theta)]}{N-1} \;\xrightarrow[\;N\to\infty\;]{}\; \mathrm{Var}(\theta) \,>\,0.$$

This is the punchline of the whole problem, so let's say it slowly. Put this next to Question 1(c). Same gigantic box. There, we knew $\theta$, so $\mathrm{Var}(\theta)=0$, and the covariance went to zero — independent. Here we don't know $\theta$, so $\mathrm{Var}(\theta)$ stays stubbornly positive. A big box washes away the physics, but it does nothing about your ignorance. Only knowing $\theta$ can do that. So 1(c) gets to be (almost) independent and 2(c) never does — and the single thing that separates them is whether you know what's in the box.

3. All Six Cases in One Line

Every single scenario from both pages is hiding inside one formula. "Put it back" just drops the second term; a huge box sends it to zero:

$$\mathrm{Cov}(y_1,y_2) \;=\; \underbrace{\mathrm{Var}(\theta)}_{\text{learning}} \;-\; \underbrace{\frac{E[\theta(1-\theta)]}{N-1}\,\cdot\,\mathbb{1}\{\text{without replacement}\}}_{\text{depletion}}$$

Two separate knobs. The box size $N$ only touches the depletion term. Knowing $\theta$ only touches the learning term. To get true independence you have to zero out both — and that essentially never happens unless you already know the answer.

4. de Finetti's Theorem — the Surprise Run in Reverse

Up to now I sneaked in a prior $p(\theta)$ and a conditional-coin-flip story, and we computed away. de Finetti's theorem does something far more impressive: it runs the whole thing backward. You don't have to assume any of that machinery. Just say "I'm willing to treat my observations as exchangeable, forever" — and the theorem hands you the parameter, the prior, and the independence-given-$\theta$ structure, all for free.

The theorem (for our coin-flip / black-or-white case)

Take any infinitely exchangeable sequence of $0$/$1$ draws $y_1, y_2, \dots$. Then there is one and only one distribution $p(\theta)$ on $[0,1]$ for which, for every $n$ (with $s=\sum_i y_i$ blacks),

$$p(y_1,\dots,y_n) = \int_0^1 \prod_{i=1}^n \theta^{y_i}(1-\theta)^{1-y_i}\; p(\theta)\,d\theta = \int_0^1 \theta^{s}(1-\theta)^{n-s}\,p(\theta)\,d\theta.$$

And $\theta$ isn't some abstract gadget — it's literally the long-run fraction of black you'd see if you drew forever, $\theta = \displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n y_i$, and $p(\theta)$ is just the distribution of that limit.

Read it again slowly, because it's saying three astonishing things:

  1. A parameter has to exist. We never put $\theta$ in by hand — the theorem builds it for us, as the long-run frequency. Exchangeability alone guarantees that limit is there.
  2. Given $\theta$, the draws are independent coins. The thing inside the integral is a plain product — exactly Definition 4 from the last page.
  3. That $p(\theta)$ is a genuine prior. It's the one and only mixing distribution — your uncertainty about the long-run frequency, before any data.
Why it has to be infinite exchangeability — and our negative number proves it

Remember the minus sign we tucked away in Question 1? Time to collect. Anything built the de Finetti way — pick $\theta$, then draw independently — must have, by Tool 2,

$$\mathrm{Cov}(y_1,y_2) = \mathrm{Var}(\theta) \ge 0.$$

But the finite "keep it out" box was perfectly exchangeable and had $\mathrm{Cov}=-\dfrac{\theta(1-\theta)}{N-1}<0$. A negative number can never equal a variance. So that box, nice as it is, cannot have been built by mixing independent draws — which means it can't be stretched into an infinite exchangeable sequence at all. Plain finite exchangeability is genuinely weaker, and that's the exact gap de Finetti's theorem closes by demanding the infinite version. (And the huge-box limit is precisely where that pesky negative term dies and the theorem becomes usable again.)

If you want it concrete: the two-ball box has $\mathrm{Cov}=-\tfrac14$. No prior $p(\theta)$ on Earth can make two conditionally-independent draws anti-correlated. It simply can't be done.

5. So Why Are We Allowed to Use Hierarchical Models?

Now look at the theorem's formula and look at how we actually write down a Bayesian model, and notice they're the very same thing wearing different clothes:

$$\underbrace{p(y_1,\dots,y_n) = \int_0^1 \Big[\textstyle\prod_i p(y_i\mid\theta)\Big]\,p(\theta)\,d\theta}_{\text{what de Finetti gives you}} \;\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\; \underbrace{\theta\sim p(\theta),\quad y_i\mid\theta \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} p(y\mid\theta)}_{\text{a hierarchical model}}$$

They're identical. And that's a strong statement: the moment you're willing to call your observations exchangeable, you've already signed up for a hierarchical model with a parameter $\theta$ and a prior $p(\theta)$. The prior isn't some optional Bayesian decoration you could argue away — the theorem says it has to be there. That's our license, in this chapter, for treating a bunch of rats, or schools, or factories as draws from one shared population.

And now the role of not knowing $\theta$ snaps into focus. That dependence we worked so hard to compute, $\mathrm{Cov}(y_1,y_2)=\mathrm{Var}(\theta)$, is not a nuisance — it's the actual pipe through which learning flows:

$$p(\theta \mid y_1) \;\propto\; p(y_1\mid\theta)\,p(\theta) \;\neq\; p(\theta) \quad\Longrightarrow\quad p(y_2\mid y_1) \neq p(y_2).$$

If the draws were truly independent — $\theta$ known, $\mathrm{Var}(\theta)=0$ — then seeing $y_1$ wouldn't move your belief about $\theta$ one inch, and data would teach you nothing. Turn it around: the draws are dependent precisely because $\theta$ is unknown, and that dependence is exactly what lets one observation inform the next. Pooling information across exchangeable units works because they're linked, and they're linked because there's a shared unknown.

A concrete one to hold — the Beta–Binomial

Put $p(\theta)=\mathrm{Beta}(\alpha,\beta)$. Then $E[\theta]=\frac{\alpha}{\alpha+\beta}$, and the covariance between two draws is just

$$\mathrm{Cov}(y_1,y_2) = \mathrm{Var}(\theta) = \frac{\alpha\beta}{(\alpha+\beta)^2(\alpha+\beta+1)} \;>\;0.$$

Take a flat prior $\mathrm{Beta}(1,1)$ and you get $\mathrm{Var}(\theta)=\tfrac{1}{12}\approx0.083$ — that's a real, sizable link, manufactured from nothing but "I don't know." Then as data pile in, the posterior on $\theta$ tightens, $\mathrm{Var}(\theta\mid\text{data})\to 0$, and future draws drift toward independence. That's the rat-tumor model of this chapter in a nutshell: a $\mathrm{Beta}$ population prior whose knobs are themselves learned across a whole set of exchangeable experiments.

6. The Whole Story in One Breath

Calling your observations exchangeable is really just a statement about the symmetry of your own ignorance. By de Finetti, that one modest judgment forces a parameter $\theta$ into existence (the long-run frequency), hands you a prior $p(\theta)$, and makes the draws independent once $\theta$ is known — which is to say, it hands you a hierarchical model. While $\theta$ stays unknown the draws are linked, $\mathrm{Cov}(y_1,y_2)=\mathrm{Var}(\theta)>0$, and that very link is what lets one draw update your belief about $\theta$ and therefore about the next draw. Hand someone the true $\theta$ and the link vanishes — back to independence. And no pile of extra data and no enormous box can ever substitute for knowing $\theta$ — which is the whole reason that, whenever the parameter is unknown, the right tool is a hierarchical model and not a shrug toward independence.
