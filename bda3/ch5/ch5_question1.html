Exchangeability vs. Independence

Deconstructing the fundamental assumptions of statistical modeling through three intuitive thought experiments.

The Definitions: First Principles

Definition: Exchangeability

The "Symmetry" Principle. A sequence of random variables is exchangeable if their joint probability distribution is invariant to permutations of their indices.

$$p(y_1, y_2) = p(y_2, y_1)$$

It means the "labels" (1st, 2nd) carry no information. The order in which we observe the data doesn't affect our belief about the underlying distribution.

Definition: Independence

The "Information Isolation" Principle. Two variables are independent if knowing the outcome of one tells you absolutely nothing about the outcome of the other.

$$p(y_1, y_2) = p(y_1)p(y_2)$$ $$p(y_2 | y_1) = p(y_2)$$

Independence is a much stronger condition than exchangeability. It allows us to multiply probabilities directly.

The Golden Rule: Independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) implies exchangeability. However, exchangeability does not imply independence!

Analyzing the Scenarios

A

With Replacement (Small Population)

"A box has one black ball and one white ball. We pick a ball $y_1$ at random, put it back, and pick another ball $y_2$ at random."

(i) Are they Exchangeable?

Yes. The order doesn't matter. The probability of picking (Black, White) is $\frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4}$. The probability of picking (White, Black) is also $\frac{1}{4}$. Symmetry holds perfectly.

(ii) Are they Independent?

Yes. Because we put the ball back (replacement), the state of the box is identical for the second draw. Knowing $y_1$ gives absolutely zero information about $y_2$.

(iii) Can we Act Independent?

Yes. We don't just "act" like they are independent; they are mathematically strictly independent. We can blindly use the multiplication rule.

B

Without Replacement (Small Population)

"A box has one black ball and one white ball. We pick a ball $y_1$ at random, we do not put it back, then we pick ball $y_2$."

(i) Are they Exchangeable?

Yes! This is counter-intuitive, but let's check symmetry: $p(y_1=B, y_2=W) = p(B)p(W|B) = \frac{1}{2} \times 1 = \frac{1}{2}$.
$p(y_1=W, y_2=B) = p(W)p(B|W) = \frac{1}{2} \times 1 = \frac{1}{2}$.
Since $p(y_1, y_2) = p(y_2, y_1)$, they are perfectly exchangeable.

(ii) Are they Independent?

No. Knowing $y_1$ gives you perfect information about $y_2$. If $y_1$ is Black, $y_2$ MUST be White. Therefore, $p(y_2 | y_1) \neq p(y_2)$. They are highly dependent.

(iii) Can we Act Independent?

Absolutely Not. If we acted as if they were independent, we would calculate $p(y_1=B, y_2=B) = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4}$. But in reality, this outcome is impossible ($p=0$). Assuming independence here creates fatal modeling errors.

C

Without Replacement (Vast Population)

"A box has a million black balls and a million white balls. We pick a ball $y_1$ at random, we do not put it back, then we pick ball $y_2$."

(i) Are they Exchangeable?

Yes. Just like scenario B, symmetry holds completely. The joint probability of sequence $(B, W)$ is exactly identical to the sequence $(W, B)$.

(ii) Are they Independent?

Technically No. Because we don't replace the ball, $y_1$ slightly alters the box. If $y_1=B$, the chance of $y_2=B$ drops from $0.5$ to $\frac{999,999}{1,999,999} \approx 0.4999997$. Because $0.4999997 \neq 0.5$, they are not strictly independent.

(iii) Can we Act Independent?

Yes! This is the essence of asymptotic approximation. The tiny dependency ($0.5$ vs $0.4999997$) is entirely negligible for any practical inference. In large populations, "without replacement" converges to "with replacement" behavior. We can act as if they are i.i.d.

The "Why" Behind the Math

In Bayesian statistics, exchangeability is the fundamental assumption we need to build hierarchical models and apply de Finetti's theorem. De Finetti proved that if observations are exchangeable, we can model them as if they are independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) conditional on some underlying, unobserved parameters.

Scenario (B) highlights why exchangeability is more robust than independence: it captures the symmetry of our ignorance. Even when data are highly dependent, if we have no prior reason to distinguish between the order of observations, we can leverage exchangeability to define a joint prior distribution.
