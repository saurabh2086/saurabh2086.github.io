Chapter 5 • Question 1
Let's Figure It Out

Two Balls Out of a Box

Suppose I know exactly what's in the box. I pull out two balls and ask a simple question: does the first one tell me anything about the second? It sounds trivial, and in one case it is. But by chasing the arithmetic honestly we'll stumble onto something strange — a negative number that, later on, turns out to be impossible to fake. That little surprise is the whole reason de Finetti's theorem has to be careful.

1. First, Let's Agree on What the Words Mean

Before we can argue about anything we'd better nail down the words, because half of all confusion in this subject is people using "independent" and "exchangeable" as if they were the same thing. They are not. Let each draw be a number $y_i$ that is $1$ if the ball is black and $0$ if it's white, and let $p(\cdot)$ be the probability of whatever combination we're talking about. Here are the four ideas we need, and that's all we need.

Definition 1 — Finite exchangeability

The draws $y_1, \dots, y_n$ are exchangeable if shuffling their order doesn't change the probability of anything. For every reordering $\pi$,

$$p(y_1, \dots, y_n) = p(y_{\pi(1)}, \dots, y_{\pi(n)}).$$

In plain words: the labels "first" and "second" are bookkeeping. Nature doesn't care which one we wrote down first.

Definition 2 — Infinite exchangeability

Now imagine you could keep drawing forever, and every finite batch you ever look at is exchangeable. That's infinite exchangeability, and it's a genuinely stronger demand than the finite kind. Keep an eye on this one — it's the fine print that de Finetti's theorem insists on, and we'll see exactly why it matters.

Definition 3 — Independence

The draws are independent if the probability of the whole batch is just the probabilities multiplied together:

$$p(y_1, \dots, y_n) = \prod_{i=1}^n p(y_i).$$

Same thing as saying $p(y_2 \mid y_1) = p(y_2)$: knowing the first draw tells you absolutely nothing new about the second. Nothing.

Definition 4 — Independence once you're told $\theta$

And here's the sneaky one. The draws are independent given $\theta$ if, once somebody whispers the value of $\theta$ in your ear, the multiplication rule comes back:

$$p(y_1, \dots, y_n \mid \theta) = \prod_{i=1}^n p(y_i \mid \theta).$$

How these stack up. Each arrow below is true, and — this is the important part — not one of them runs backward:

$$\text{i.i.d.} \;\Longrightarrow\; \text{independent} \;\Longrightarrow\; \text{exchangeable}.$$

So exchangeable is the weakest, most forgiving condition of the three. In a minute (scenario b) we'll meet a pair that's exchangeable but not independent. Over in Question 2 we'll meet pairs that aren't independent yet are built out of independent pieces. Keeping these straight is the entire game.

2. The Box

Here's our gadget. A box with $N$ balls in it, $K$ of them black, the rest white. And the key thing for this whole page: we know $N$ and $K$. No mystery about what's inside. Call the fraction of black balls

$$\theta \,=\, \frac{K}{N} \in [0,1], \qquad \theta \text{ is a plain number we already know.}$$

We pull two balls and write down $y_1$, then $y_2$. The three scenarios are all the same box — the only thing we change is the drawing procedure. And notice this, because it's going to organize everything: since we already know $\theta$, there is nothing left to learn from the data. So if the two draws turn out to be related at all, it can only be because the physical act of pulling a ball did something to the box. That's the only suspect we have.

A

Put It Back

Pull $y_1$, drop it back in, shake, then pull $y_2$.

This is the easy one, so let's warm up on it. Start with a single pull. Because you put the ball back, the box for the second pull is identical to the first, and each pull is just a coin with weight $\theta$:

$$P(y_i = 1) = \theta, \qquad P(y_i = 0) = 1-\theta.$$

Here's a slick way to write those two facts as one formula. Notice that

$$p(y_i) = \theta^{\,y_i}(1-\theta)^{\,1-y_i}$$

does exactly the right thing: plug in $y_i=1$ and you get $\theta^1(1-\theta)^0=\theta$; plug in $y_i=0$ and you get $\theta^0(1-\theta)^1=1-\theta$. The exponents are just switches that turn each factor on or off. Now, because the draws are independent (you restored the box), the probability of the pair is the product of the two single-draw probabilities:

$$p(y_1, y_2) = \big[\theta^{\,y_1}(1-\theta)^{\,1-y_1}\big]\big[\theta^{\,y_2}(1-\theta)^{\,1-y_2}\big] = \theta^{\,y_1+y_2}\,(1-\theta)^{\,2-y_1-y_2},$$

where in the last step we just added the exponents. If you'd rather see it as a plain table, it's the same four numbers: $p(1,1)=\theta^2$, $\ p(1,0)=p(0,1)=\theta(1-\theta)$, $\ p(0,0)=(1-\theta)^2$.

(i) Exchangeable?

Sure. Look at the formula — it only cares about the sum $y_1 + y_2$, and a sum doesn't notice which order you added the numbers. So $p(y_1,y_2)=p(y_2,y_1)$, and you could keep this up forever, so it's even infinitely exchangeable.

(ii) Independent?

Yes, honestly independent. The formula splits cleanly into one piece for the first ball and one for the second, $p(y_1,y_2)=p(y_1)p(y_2)$ — which is the definition, no fudging.

(iii) Now compute the covariance

Covariance just measures whether two things tend to move together. First the easy marginal, $E[y_i] = 1\cdot\theta + 0\cdot(1-\theta) = \theta$. The piece worth proving rather than asserting is the product $E[y_1 y_2]$. Use the same trick we'll lean on throughout: since each $y_i$ is $0$ or $1$, the product is $1$ only when both balls are black, so its average is just the probability of two blacks. Write it as a sum over the four outcomes and watch three vanish:

$$\begin{aligned} E[y_1 y_2] &= \sum_{a\in\{0,1\}}\sum_{b\in\{0,1\}} a\,b\;p(y_1=a,\,y_2=b)\\[4pt] &= 1\cdot p(1,1) \qquad(\text{only the }a=b=1\text{ term survives})\\[4pt] &= p(y_1=1)\,p(y_2=1) \qquad(\text{independent})\\[4pt] &= \theta\cdot\theta = \theta^2. \end{aligned}$$

Now drop both pieces into the covariance:

$$\mathrm{Cov}(y_1,y_2) = E[y_1y_2]-E[y_1]E[y_2] = \theta^2 - \theta\cdot\theta = 0.$$

Zero, exactly as it should be — there's no link between the draws, so use the multiplication rule and don't lose sleep.

B

Keep It Out

Pull $y_1$, set it aside, then pull $y_2$ from what's left.

Now it gets interesting, because the first ball you remove actually changes the box. Let's just write down the probability of each of the four outcomes, step by step, using "probability of the first times probability of the second given the first":

$$\begin{aligned} p(1,1) &= \tfrac{K}{N}\cdot\tfrac{K-1}{N-1}, &\quad p(1,0) &= \tfrac{K}{N}\cdot\tfrac{N-K}{N-1},\\[4pt] p(0,1) &= \tfrac{N-K}{N}\cdot\tfrac{K}{N-1}, &\quad p(0,0) &= \tfrac{N-K}{N}\cdot\tfrac{N-K-1}{N-1}. \end{aligned}$$

(i) Exchangeable?

Yes — and this surprises people. "How can it be order-independent when removing a ball clearly changes things?" Fair question. So let's just check it. Compare the two mixed outcomes:

$$p(1,0)=\frac{K(N-K)}{N(N-1)} = p(0,1).$$

They're equal! And the all-black and all-white cases are obviously symmetric. So shuffling the order changes nothing. Let me push one step further and check that the second draw, all by itself, is black with the same chance as the first:

$$P(y_2=1) = \tfrac{K}{N}\tfrac{K-1}{N-1} + \tfrac{N-K}{N}\tfrac{K}{N-1} = \frac{K\big[(K-1)+(N-K)\big]}{N(N-1)} = \frac{K(N-1)}{N(N-1)} = \frac{K}{N} = \theta.$$

Beautiful — $E[y_1]=E[y_2]=\theta$, even though the draws lean on each other. That's exchangeability for you: symmetric, but not independent.

(ii) Independent?

No, not at all. If the first ball came out black, there's one fewer black left, so the chance the second is black drops from $\tfrac{K}{N}$ to $\tfrac{K-1}{N-1}$. Those aren't equal, so the first draw genuinely tells you something. They're tied together.

(iii) Put a number on it — the covariance

Let's grind it out and see how strong the link is. Plug $E[y_1y_2]=p(1,1)=\tfrac{K}{N}\tfrac{K-1}{N-1}$ and $E[y_i]=\theta=\tfrac{K}{N}$ into the covariance and simplify:

$$\begin{aligned} \mathrm{Cov}(y_1,y_2) &= \frac{K}{N}\cdot\frac{K-1}{N-1} - \left(\frac{K}{N}\right)^2 = \frac{K}{N}\left(\frac{K-1}{N-1}-\frac{K}{N}\right)\\[6pt] &= \frac{K}{N}\cdot\frac{N(K-1)-K(N-1)}{N(N-1)} = \frac{K}{N}\cdot\frac{K-N}{N(N-1)}\\[6pt] &= -\,\frac{K(N-K)}{N^2(N-1)} = -\,\frac{\theta(1-\theta)}{N-1}. \end{aligned}$$

And there's the surprise I promised: the covariance is negative. It makes perfect physical sense — grab a black ball and you've used one up, so the second is a touch less likely to be black. The draws repel each other a little. Quick check with the tiniest box, $N=2,\ K=1\ (\theta=\tfrac12)$: the formula says $-\tfrac14$, and indeed two blacks is flat-out impossible there, which is as anti-correlated as you can get. Good. Tuck that minus sign away; it comes back to haunt us in Question 2.

C

Keep It Out — But the Box Is Enormous

Same as (B), but now there are heaps of each color — say a million black and a million white — and we still know $\theta$.

(i) Exchangeable?

Yes — none of the algebra in (B) cared how big $N$ was, so it still goes through.

(ii) Independent, and what happens to the covariance?

Strictly speaking, still no. But here's the thing — we already have the exact answer from (B), and all we have to do is watch what it does as the box gets huge:

$$\mathrm{Cov}(y_1,y_2) = -\,\frac{\theta(1-\theta)}{N-1} \;\xrightarrow[\;N\to\infty\;]{}\; 0.$$

With a million of each, $N=2{,}000{,}000,\ \theta=\tfrac12$, and the covariance is $-\dfrac{1/4}{1{,}999{,}999} \approx -1.25\times10^{-7}$. Removing one ball nudges the odds from $0.5$ to $0.499999\ldots$ — who cares? So you may happily pretend the draws are independent. When the box is gigantic, "keep it out" and "put it back" become the same thing for all practical purposes.

3. What We Found, and the Loose Thread

(a) Put it backYesYes$0$Yes (for real)
(b) Keep it outYesNo$-\dfrac{\theta(1-\theta)}{N-1}$No
(c) Keep it out, huge boxYesNo (strictly)$\to 0$Yes (close enough)

So all three are exchangeable, but only the first is truly independent. That's our concrete proof that exchangeable does not mean independent — they really are different ideas. And the only knob we turned was the size of the box, which only ever weakens the physical link from removing a ball.

Hang on to this one thing

In case (b) the covariance came out negative. That seems like a small thing. It isn't. In Question 2 we'll prove that any sequence you build by picking a $\theta$ at random and then drawing independently must have

$$\mathrm{Cov}(y_1,y_2) = \mathrm{Var}(\theta) \;\ge\; 0.$$

A variance can't be negative. So our perfectly nice, perfectly exchangeable "keep it out" box is something that simply cannot be built that way — and that means it can't be stretched into an infinite exchangeable sequence either. That is the whole reason de Finetti's theorem has to demand infinite exchangeability and not just the finite kind. And notice the escape hatch: blow the box up to infinity (case c) and the troublesome minus sign melts to zero, so the construction becomes legal again.

On to Question 2, where we stop knowing what's in the box. The covariance flips positive, and the whole setup quietly turns into a hierarchical model — with de Finetti's theorem doing the magic.
