Exercise 5.1 Exchangeability vs. Independence

Deconstructing the fundamental assumptions of statistical modeling through three intuitive thought experiments.

The Definitions: First Principles

Definition: Exchangeability

The "Symmetry" Principle. A sequence of random variables is exchangeable if their joint probability distribution is invariant to permutations of their indices.

$$p(y_1, y_2) = p(y_2, y_1)$$

It means the "labels" (1st, 2nd) carry no information. The order in which we observe the data doesn't affect our belief about the underlying distribution.

Definition: Independence

The "Information Isolation" Principle. Two variables are independent if knowing the outcome of one tells you absolutely nothing about the outcome of the other.

$$p(y_1, y_2) = p(y_1)p(y_2)$$ $$p(y_2 | y_1) = p(y_2)$$

Independence is a much stronger condition than exchangeability. It allows us to multiply probabilities directly.

First Principle: Covariance of Binary Indicators

To mathematically quantify dependency, we calculate the covariance of two binary random variables $y_1, y_2 \in \{0, 1\}$. By definition, $E[y_i] = 1 \cdot P(y_i = 1) + 0 \cdot P(y_i = 0) = P(y_i = 1)$. Similarly, since the only non-zero product of the variables is $y_1 y_2 = 1 \times 1 = 1$, we have $E[y_1 y_2] = P(y_1 = 1, y_2 = 1)$. Thus, the covariance simplifies directly to:

$$\begin{aligned} \mathrm{Cov}(y_1, y_2) &= E[y_1 y_2] - E[y_1]E[y_2] \\[4pt] &= P(y_1=1, y_2=1) - P(y_1=1)P(y_2=1) \end{aligned}$$

Derivation for Drawing Without Replacement (Population size $N$, known fraction $\theta$)

If a box contains $N$ balls, out of which a fraction $\theta$ are black (total $N\theta$ black balls), we draw two balls without replacement:

  1. The marginal probability of drawing a black ball on any draw is $E[y_1] = E[y_2] = \theta$.
  2. The expected value of their product is the joint probability of drawing two black balls: $$E[y_1 y_2] = P(y_1=1, y_2=1) = P(y_1=1)P(y_2=1 \mid y_1=1) = \theta \cdot \frac{N\theta - 1}{N - 1}$$
  3. Plugging these into the covariance formula yields: $$\begin{aligned} \mathrm{Cov}(y_1, y_2) &= E[y_1 y_2] - E[y_1]E[y_2] \\[6pt] &= \theta \cdot \frac{N\theta - 1}{N - 1} - \theta^2 \\[6pt] &= \theta \left( \frac{N\theta - 1 - \theta(N - 1)}{N - 1} \right) \\[6pt] &= \theta \left( \frac{N\theta - 1 - N\theta + \theta}{N - 1} \right) \\[6pt] &= \theta \left( \frac{\theta - 1}{N - 1} \right) \\[6pt] &= -\frac{\theta(1 - \theta)}{N - 1} \end{aligned}$$

If $y_1$ and $y_2$ are independent, $P(y_1=1, y_2=1) = P(y_1=1)P(y_2=1)$, yielding a covariance of zero. A non-zero covariance indicates statistical dependence.

The Golden Rule: Independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) implies exchangeability. However, exchangeability does not imply independence!

Analyzing the Scenarios

A

With Replacement (Small Population)

"A box has one black ball and one white ball. We pick a ball $y_1$ at random, put it back, and pick another ball $y_2$ at random."

(i) Are they Exchangeable?

Yes. We show that the joint probability is symmetric, i.e., $p(y_1, y_2) = p(y_2, y_1)$ for all $y_1, y_2 \in \{0, 1\}$ (where $1$ is black and $0$ is white). Using the general product rule $p(y_1, y_2) = p(y_1)p(y_2 \mid y_1)$:

$$\begin{aligned} p(y_1=1, y_2=1) &= p(y_1=1)p(y_2=1 \mid y_1=1) = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4} \\[4pt] p(y_1=0, y_2=0) &= p(y_1=0)p(y_2=0 \mid y_1=0) = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4} \\[4pt] p(y_1=1, y_2=0) &= p(y_1=1)p(y_2=0 \mid y_1=1) = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4} \\[4pt] p(y_1=0, y_2=1) &= p(y_1=0)p(y_2=1 \mid y_1=0) = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4} \end{aligned}$$

Note: Since drawing is with replacement, the second draw is independent of the first, so $p(y_2 \mid y_1) = p(y_2) = \frac{1}{2}$ in all cases.

Verifying the symmetry $p(y_1, y_2) = p(y_2, y_1)$ for all pairs:

  • For $(1, 1)$: $p(y_1=1, y_2=1) = p(y_2=1, y_1=1) = \frac{1}{4}$
  • For $(0, 0)$: $p(y_1=0, y_2=0) = p(y_2=0, y_1=0) = \frac{1}{4}$
  • For $(1, 0)$: $p(y_1=1, y_2=0) = \frac{1}{4}$ and $p(y_2=1, y_1=0) = p(y_1=0, y_2=1) = \frac{1}{4}$
  • For $(0, 1)$: $p(y_1=0, y_2=1) = \frac{1}{4}$ and $p(y_2=0, y_1=1) = p(y_1=1, y_2=0) = \frac{1}{4}$

Since the joint probability is invariant to permutations in all cases, the sequence is exchangeable.

(ii) Are they Independent?

Yes. Because we put the ball back (replacement), the state of the box is identical for the second draw. Mathematically, the covariance is exactly zero: $$\mathrm{Cov}(y_1, y_2) = P(y_1=1, y_2=1) - P(y_1=1)P(y_2=1) = \frac{1}{4} - \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) = 0$$ Knowing $y_1$ gives absolutely zero information about $y_2$.

(iii) Can we Act Independent?

Yes. We don't just "act" like they are independent; they are mathematically strictly independent. We can blindly use the multiplication rule.

B

Without Replacement (Small Population)

"A box has one black ball and one white ball. We pick a ball $y_1$ at random, we do not put it back, then we pick ball $y_2$."

(i) Are they Exchangeable?

Yes. To prove exchangeability, we must show that the joint probability is symmetric, i.e., $p(y_1, y_2) = p(y_2, y_1)$ for all possible outcomes of $y_1, y_2 \in \{0, 1\}$ (where $1$ is black and $0$ is white). Let's expand the joint probabilities for all 4 combinations:

$$\begin{aligned} p(y_1=1, y_2=1) &= p(y_1=1)p(y_2=1 \mid y_1=1) = \frac{1}{2} \times 0 = 0 \\[4pt] p(y_1=0, y_2=0) &= p(y_1=0)p(y_2=0 \mid y_1=0) = \frac{1}{2} \times 0 = 0 \\[4pt] p(y_1=1, y_2=0) &= p(y_1=1)p(y_2=0 \mid y_1=1) = \frac{1}{2} \times 1 = \frac{1}{2} \\[4pt] p(y_1=0, y_2=1) &= p(y_1=0)p(y_2=1 \mid y_1=0) = \frac{1}{2} \times 1 = \frac{1}{2} \end{aligned}$$

Now let's verify if the symmetry $p(y_1, y_2) = p(y_2, y_1)$ holds for each case:

  • For $(1, 1)$: $p(y_1=1, y_2=1) = p(y_2=1, y_1=1) = 0$
  • For $(0, 0)$: $p(y_1=0, y_2=0) = p(y_2=0, y_1=0) = 0$
  • For $(1, 0)$: $p(y_1=1, y_2=0) = \frac{1}{2}$ and $p(y_2=1, y_1=0) = p(y_1=0, y_2=1) = \frac{1}{2}$
  • For $(0, 1)$: $p(y_1=0, y_2=1) = \frac{1}{2}$ and $p(y_2=0, y_1=1) = p(y_1=1, y_2=0) = \frac{1}{2}$

Since the joint probability is invariant to permutations in all cases, the sequence is exchangeable.

(ii) Are they Independent?

No. Knowing $y_1$ gives you perfect information about $y_2$. Mathematically, the covariance is negative: $$\mathrm{Cov}(y_1, y_2) = P(y_1=1, y_2=1) - P(y_1=1)P(y_2=1) = 0 - \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) = -0.25$$ Since the covariance is non-zero (specifically, negative due to physical depletion), they are highly dependent.

(iii) Can we Act Independent?

Absolutely Not. If we acted as if they were independent, we would calculate $p(y_1=B, y_2=B) = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4}$. But in reality, this outcome is impossible ($p=0$). Assuming independence here creates fatal modeling errors.

C

Without Replacement (Vast Population)

"A box has a million black balls and a million white balls. We pick a ball $y_1$ at random, we do not put it back, then we pick ball $y_2$."

(i) Are they Exchangeable?

Yes. For $N = 2,000,000$ balls ($B = 1,000,000$ black, $W = 1,000,000$ white), drawing without replacement gives:

$$\begin{aligned} p(y_1=1, y_2=1) &= p(y_1=1)p(y_2=1 \mid y_1=1) = \frac{1,000,000}{2,000,000} \times \frac{999,999}{1,999,999} = \frac{999,999}{3,999,998} \\[4pt] p(y_1=0, y_2=0) &= p(y_1=0)p(y_2=0 \mid y_1=0) = \frac{1,000,000}{2,000,000} \times \frac{999,999}{1,999,999} = \frac{999,999}{3,999,998} \\[4pt] p(y_1=1, y_2=0) &= p(y_1=1)p(y_2=0 \mid y_1=1) = \frac{1,000,000}{2,000,000} \times \frac{1,000,000}{1,999,999} = \frac{1,000,000}{3,999,998} \\[4pt] p(y_1=0, y_2=1) &= p(y_1=0)p(y_2=1 \mid y_1=0) = \frac{1,000,000}{2,000,000} \times \frac{1,000,000}{1,999,999} = \frac{1,000,000}{3,999,998} \end{aligned}$$

Verifying the symmetry $p(y_1, y_2) = p(y_2, y_1)$ for all pairs:

  • For $(1, 1)$: $p(y_1=1, y_2=1) = p(y_2=1, y_1=1) = \frac{999,999}{3,999,998}$
  • For $(0, 0)$: $p(y_1=0, y_2=0) = p(y_2=0, y_1=0) = \frac{999,999}{3,999,998}$
  • For $(1, 0)$: $p(y_1=1, y_2=0) = \frac{1,000,000}{3,999,998}$ and $p(y_2=1, y_1=0) = p(y_1=0, y_2=1) = \frac{1,000,000}{3,999,998}$
  • For $(0, 1)$: $p(y_1=0, y_2=1) = \frac{1,000,000}{3,999,998}$ and $p(y_2=0, y_1=1) = p(y_1=1, y_2=0) = \frac{1,000,000}{3,999,998}$

Just as in Scenario B, the joint distribution is fully symmetric under index permutation, proving that they are exchangeable.

(ii) Are they Independent?

Technically No. Because we don't replace the ball, $y_1$ slightly alters the box. With $N = 2,000,000$ and $\theta = 0.5$: $$\mathrm{Cov}(y_1, y_2) = -\frac{\theta(1-\theta)}{N-1} = -\frac{0.25}{1,999,999} \approx -1.25 \times 10^{-7}$$ Because the covariance is not strictly zero, they are not mathematically independent.

(iii) Can we Act Independent?

Yes! This is the essence of asymptotic approximation. The tiny dependency ($0.5$ vs $0.4999997$) is entirely negligible for any practical inference. In large populations, "without replacement" converges to "with replacement" behavior. We can act as if they are i.i.d.

The "Why" Behind the Math

In Bayesian statistics, exchangeability is the fundamental assumption we need to build hierarchical models and apply de Finetti's theorem. De Finetti proved that if observations are exchangeable, we can model them as if they are independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) conditional on some underlying, unobserved parameters.

Scenario (B) highlights why exchangeability is more robust than independence: it captures the symmetry of our ignorance. Even when data are highly dependent, if we have no prior reason to distinguish between the order of observations, we can leverage exchangeability to define a joint prior distribution.

Quantifying It: The Covariance

Each "independent or not?" answer can be made exact by computing $\mathrm{Cov}(y_1, y_2)$. Let $y_i = 1$ for black, $0$ for white. Here the black fraction $\theta$ is known, so there is nothing to learn — any dependence can only come from the physical draw mechanism.

For a box of $N$ balls with known black fraction $\theta$, drawing without replacement:

$$\begin{aligned} \mathrm{Cov}(y_1, y_2) &= E[y_1 y_2] - E[y_1]\,E[y_2] \\[4pt] &= \theta\cdot\frac{N\theta - 1}{N - 1} - \theta^2 \\[4pt] &= -\,\frac{\theta(1-\theta)}{N - 1} \end{aligned}$$

Here $E[y_1 y_2] = P(\text{both black}) = \dfrac{N\theta}{N}\cdot\dfrac{N\theta - 1}{N - 1}$. The covariance is negative: removing a black ball makes a second black ball less likely (depletion).

Plugging in each scenario (here $\theta = \tfrac12$):

  • (a) With replacement: no depletion, $\mathrm{Cov} = 0$ — strictly independent.
  • (b) Without replacement, $N=2$: $\mathrm{Cov} = -\dfrac{(1/2)(1/2)}{2-1} = -\dfrac14$ — strongly dependent.
  • (c) Without replacement, $N=2{,}000{,}000$: $\mathrm{Cov} = -\dfrac{1/4}{1{,}999{,}999} \approx -1.3\times10^{-7}$ — negligible, so we can act independent.

Notice the dependence vanishes as $N \to \infty$. This is the entire content of (c): a vast population washes out the only source of dependence available when $\theta$ is known. The moment $\theta$ becomes unknown (Question 2), a second, positive term $\mathrm{Var}(\theta)$ appears that no population size can remove.
