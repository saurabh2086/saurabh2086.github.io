5.6 Hierarchical Modeling Applied to a Meta-Analysis

Applying the hierarchical normal model to combine and synthesize treatment effects from several parallel clinical trials in medicine.

Meta-analysis is an increasingly popular and important process of summarizing and integrating the findings of research studies in a particular area. As a method for combining information from several parallel data sources, meta-analysis is closely connected to hierarchical modeling. In this section we consider a relatively simple application of hierarchical modeling to a meta-analysis in medicine. (We consider another meta-analysis problem in the context of a decision problem in Section 9.2.)

The data in our medical example are displayed in the first three columns of Table 5.4, which summarize mortality after myocardial infarction in 22 clinical trials, each consisting of two groups of heart attack patients randomly allocated to receive or not receive beta-blockers (a family of drugs that affect the central nervous system and can relax the heart muscles). Mortality varies from 3% to 21% across the studies, most of which show a modest, though not 'statistically significant,' benefit from the use of beta-blockers. The aim of a meta-analysis is to provide a combined analysis of the studies that indicates the overall strength of the evidence for a beneficial effect of the treatment under study. Before proceeding to a formal meta-analysis, it is important to apply rigorous criteria in determining which studies are included.

Defining a parameter for each study

In the beta-blocker example, the meta-analysis involves data in the form of several $2 \times 2$ tables. If clinical trial $j$ (in the series to be considered for meta-analysis) involves the use of $n_{0j}$ subjects in the control group and $n_{1j}$ in the treatment group, giving rise to $y_{0j}$ and $y_{1j}$ deaths in control and treatment groups, respectively, then the usual sampling model involves two independent binomial distributions: $$y_{0j} \sim \text{Binomial}(n_{0j}, p_{0j}) \quad \text{and} \quad y_{1j} \sim \text{Binomial}(n_{1j}, p_{1j})$$ where $p_{0j}$ represents the probability of death in the control group (no beta-blockers) and $p_{1j}$ represents the probability of death in the treatment group (beta-blockers) for study $j$.

When analyzing clinical trials, we want to measure how much a treatment helps. Depending on how we look at the problem, we define different estimands (the statistical quantities we want to estimate). Let's build them one by one, explaining why we care about each:

1. The Risk Difference

Why do we care?

This is the most direct, real-world measure of impact. It answers: "If I treat a group of patients, how many lives do I actually save?" For instance, if the baseline death rate in the control group is 10% ($p_{0j} = 0.10$) and the treatment cuts it to 6% ($p_{1j} = 0.06$), the difference is 4%. This tells a doctor that treating 100 patients is expected to save exactly 4 lives. It is direct, honest, and easy to explain to a patient.

$$\text{Risk Difference} = p_{1j} - p_{0j}$$
2. The Risk Ratio (Relative Risk)

Why do we care?

This measures the treatment effect relative to where you started. If your risk of dying goes from 10% to 5%, your risk is cut in half (a Risk Ratio of 0.5, or a 50% relative reduction). But if your risk goes from 0.0001% to 0.00005%, it is still cut in half, even though the absolute number of lives saved is practically zero. The risk ratio is powerful because it highlights the biological potency of the treatment, regardless of how common or rare the disease is in the population.

$$\text{Risk Ratio} = \frac{p_{1j}}{p_{0j}}$$
3. The Odds Ratio

Why do we care?

To understand the Odds Ratio, we must first look at the difference between probability and odds.

Imagine 10 patients enter a clinic, and 2 of them die. The probability of dying is 2 out of 10, or 20% ($2/10 = 0.20$). But the odds of dying are calculated by comparing those who died to those who survived: 2 died, and 8 survived. So, the odds of dying are 2 to 8, or $2/8 = 0.25$ (often expressed as 1:4).

The Odds Ratio ($\rho_j$) is simply the odds of dying in the treatment group divided by the odds of dying in the control group. We care about this measure for two main reasons:

  • The Retrospective (Case-Control) Trick: Suppose you want to study a very rare disease. If you track 10,000 healthy people forward in time (a cohort study), you might wait years for just a handful of people to get sick. That is incredibly slow and expensive. Instead, you work backward (a case-control study): you go to a hospital database, recruit 100 people who already got sick (cases) and 100 healthy people (controls), and look backward to see who took the drug.

    Because you artificially forced the study to have exactly 50% sick people, you can no longer calculate the true probability of getting sick in the real world. But the odds ratio has a magical mathematical property: the selection bias cancels itself out completely! Here is how the algebra works:

    Why the selection bias cancels out:

    Let the true population numbers of people be $N_{11}$ (Sick & Exposed), $N_{10}$ (Sick & Unexposed), $N_{01}$ (Healthy & Exposed), and $N_{00}$ (Healthy & Unexposed). The true population odds ratio of disease is: $$\text{OR}_{\text{population}} = \frac{N_{11} / N_{01}}{N_{10} / N_{00}} = \frac{N_{11} \cdot N_{00}}{N_{10} \cdot N_{01}}$$

    In your study, you select some fraction $f_{\text{case}}$ of all sick people, and some fraction $f_{\text{control}}$ of all healthy people. Because of this, the numbers in your study sample are: $$\text{Sick Exposed} = f_{\text{case}} \cdot N_{11} \quad \text{and} \quad \text{Sick Unexposed} = f_{\text{case}} \cdot N_{10}$$ $$\text{Healthy Exposed} = f_{\text{control}} \cdot N_{01} \quad \text{and} \quad \text{Healthy Unexposed} = f_{\text{control}} \cdot N_{00}$$

    If we compute the odds ratio of exposure in our study sample, we get: $$\text{OR}_{\text{sample}} = \frac{(f_{\text{case}} \cdot N_{11}) / (f_{\text{case}} \cdot N_{10})}{(f_{\text{control}} \cdot N_{01}) / (f_{\text{control}} \cdot N_{00})} = \frac{N_{11} / N_{10}}{N_{01} / N_{00}} = \frac{N_{11} \cdot N_{00}}{N_{10} \cdot N_{01}} = \text{OR}_{\text{population}}$$

    The arbitrary selection rates $f_{\text{case}}$ and $f_{\text{control}}$ completely disappear! Even though the sample is heavily biased toward sick people, the calculated Odds Ratio is exactly identical to the true population Odds Ratio.

  • Unbounded Scale: Probabilities must stay strictly between 0 and 1, which makes linear modeling (like regression) very difficult since models can predict impossible rates like 110% or -5%. Odds, however, can go from 0 to infinity. Taking the logarithm of odds expands this to all real numbers (negative to positive infinity), letting statistical models run free without hitting boundaries.
$$\rho_j = \frac{p_{1j}}{1 - p_{1j}} \Big/ \frac{p_{0j}}{1 - p_{0j}}$$
4. The Log Odds Ratio

Why do we care?

Odds ratios have an annoying mathematical asymmetry: if a drug is harmful, the odds ratio can range from $1$ to infinity. If it is beneficial, it gets squished between $0$ and $1$. This makes statistical modeling and combining studies very difficult. By taking the natural logarithm, we make the scale symmetric around $0$: positive values mean harm, negative values mean benefit, and $0$ means no effect. Most importantly, the sampling distribution of the log odds ratio is remarkably close to a normal distribution (the bell curve), which allows us to use standard normal-theory formulas for hierarchical modeling.

$$\theta_j = \log \rho_j$$

An normal approximation to the likelihood

Relatively simple Bayesian meta-analysis is possible using the normal-theory results of the previous sections if we summarize the results of each experiment $j$ with an approximate normal likelihood for the parameter $\theta_j$. This is possible with a number of standard analytic approaches that produce a point estimate and standard errors, which can be regarded as approximating a normal mean and standard deviation. One approach is based on empirical logits: for each study $j$, one can estimate $\theta_j$ by $$y_j = \log\left( \frac{y_{1j}}{n_{1j} - y_{1j}} \right) - \log\left( \frac{y_{0j}}{n_{0j} - y_{0j}} \right)$$ with approximate sampling variance $$\sigma_j^2 = \frac{1}{y_{1j}} + \frac{1}{n_{1j} - y_{1j}} + \frac{1}{y_{0j}} + \frac{1}{n_{0j} - y_{0j}}$$

We use the notation $y_j$ and $\sigma_j^2$ to be consistent with our earlier expressions for the hierarchical normal model. There are various refinements of these estimates that improve the asymptotic normality of the sampling distributions involved (in particular, it is often recommended to add a fraction such as $0.5$ to each of the four counts in the $2 \times 2$ table), but whenever study-specific sample sizes are moderately large, such details do not concern us. The estimated log-odds ratios $y_j$ and their estimated standard errors $\sigma_j$ are displayed as the fourth and fifth columns of Table 5.4.

Goals of inference in meta-analysis

Discussions of meta-analysis are sometimes imprecise about the estimands of interest in the analysis, especially when the primary focus is on testing the null hypothesis of no effect in any of the studies to be combined. Our focus is on estimating meaningful parameters, and for this objective there appear to be three possibilities:

  • Identical Replications: We view the studies as identical replications of each other, meaning we regard the individuals in all the studies as independent samples from a common population with the same outcome measures. This corresponds to the complete pooling model ($\tau = 0$).
  • Independent / Unrelated: The studies are so different that the results of any one study provide no information about the results of any of the others. This corresponds to the no pooling model ($\tau = \infty$).
  • Exchangeable (The Hierarchical Model): We regard the studies as exchangeable but not necessarily either identical or completely unrelated; we allow differences from study to study, but such that the differences are not expected *a priori* to have predictable effects favoring one study over another. This exchangeable model represents a continuum between the two extremes.

Exchangeability does not dictate the form of the joint distribution of the study effects. In what follows we adopt the convenient assumption of a normal distribution for the varying parameters; in practice it is important to check this assumption using some of the validation techniques discussed in Chapter 6.

A hierarchical normal model

A normal population distribution in conjunction with the approximate normal sampling distribution of the study-specific effect estimates allows an analysis of the same form as used for the SAT coaching example.

Hierarchical Formulation:

Stage 1 (Likelihood): $$y_j \mid \theta_j, \sigma_j \sim N(\theta_j, \sigma_j^2)$$

Stage 2 (Population Prior): $$\theta_j \mid \mu, \tau \sim N(\mu, \tau^2)$$

The estimated standard errors $\sigma_j$ are assumed known without error (this has little effect since sample sizes exceed 50 in almost all trials). We assume a noninformative uniform prior density for $\mu$ and a uniform prior density on $\tau$: $$p(\mu, \tau) \propto 1$$

Visualizing Bayesian Shrinkage (Forest Plot)

The effect of hierarchical modeling is visually demonstrated by a Forest Plot. Below, the raw observed estimates ($y_j \pm 1.96\sigma_j$) are shown in grey, and the Bayesian posterior estimates ($\theta_j$ median and 95% intervals) are shown in blue. The borrowing of strength causes the posterior intervals to shrink dramatically toward the pooled overall mean $\mu$ (dashed vertical line), especially for studies with lower sample sizes (high $\sigma_j$).

Results of the analysis and comparison to simpler methods

The analysis of our meta-analysis model now follows exactly the same methodology as in the previous sections. First, the marginal posterior density of $\tau$ peaks at a nonzero value ($\tau \approx 0.13$), although values near zero are clearly plausible, zero having a posterior density only about 25% lower than that at the mode. Posterior quantiles for the effects $\theta_j$ for the 22 studies on the logit scale are displayed as the last columns of Table 5.4.

Since the posterior distribution of $\tau$ is concentrated around values that are small relative to the sampling standard deviations of the data (compare the posterior median of $\tau$, $0.13$, in Table 5.5 to the values of $\sigma_j$ in the fourth column of Table 5.4), **considerable shrinkage is evident** in the Bayes estimates, especially for studies with low internal precision (for example, studies 1, 6, and 18). The substantial degree of homogeneity between the studies is further reflected in the large reductions in posterior variance obtained when going from the study-specific estimates to the Bayesian ones, which borrow strength from each other. Using an approximate approach fixing $\tau$ would yield standard deviations that would be too small compared to the fully Bayesian ones.

Table 5.5 gives a summary of posterior inferences for the hyperparameters $\mu$ and $\tau$ and the predicted effect, $\tilde{\theta}_j$, in a hypothetical future study. The approximate 95% highest posterior density interval for $\mu$ is $[-0.37, -0.11]$, or $[0.69, 0.90]$ when converted to the odds ratio scale (that is, exponentiated). In contrast, the 95% posterior interval that results from complete pooling—that is, assuming $\tau = 0$—is considerably narrower, $[0.70, 0.85]$.

Complete Pooling vs. Hierarchical Uncertainty: Why Model Selection Matters

Under complete pooling, the 95% interval for the odds ratio is $[0.70, 0.85]$, which was called an "unusually narrow range of uncertainty" in original debates. The hierarchical model widens this to $[0.69, 0.90]$ for the mean $\mu$, and $[0.56, 1.12]$ (on odds scale) for a **predicted new study** ($\tilde{\theta}_j$). Why does this happen?

1. The Fallacy of τ = 0

Assuming all studies are identical ($\tau = 0$) artificially inflates our sample size by pretending we have one massive, homogeneous study. If $\tau$ might actually be as high as $0.3$, we are understating our uncertainty by ignoring study-to-study variations.

2. Predicting the Future (˜θ_j)

If we want to apply this treatment to a new hospital or patient population where a study hasn't been done yet, our uncertainty isn't just about the mean effect $\mu$. It must also include the study-to-study variance $\tau^2$. When we add $\tau^2$ to our prediction interval, the uncertainty expands, showing a $\approx 10\%$ probability that the treatment could actually increase mortality ($\tilde{\theta}_j > 0$).
