Constructing a Parameterized Prior Distribution

Introduction

We begin our exploration of hierarchical models by considering a small-scale experiment. Suppose we have collected data from this current study, alongside data from several similar historical experiments.

Rather than analyzing the new experiment in isolation, we can leverage these historical studies to construct an informed prior distribution. This prior allows us to draw more robust inferences about the parameters of our current experiment.

Mathematically, this approach assumes that the underlying parameters of both the historical and current experiments are random samples drawn from a shared underlying population.

Example. Estimating the risk of tumor in a group of rats

To evaluate a drug for possible medical applications, scientists routinely perform preliminary studies on rodents. Suppose we are analyzing a particular study from the statistical literature where our immediate aim is to find the probability of a tumor, denoted as \(\theta\), in a population of laboratory rats of type 'F344' that receive a zero dose of the drug (i.e., the control group).

The data from the current experiment shows that 4 out of 14 rats developed endometrial stromal polyps.

The Model Setup

Given this data, it is natural to assume a Binomial model for the number of observed tumors \(y\), given the total number of rats \(n\) and the parameter \(\theta\):

$$y | \theta \sim \text{Binomial}(n, \theta)$$

where \(n = 14\) and \(y = 4\).

For mathematical convenience and to achieve conjugacy, we assume that the prior distribution of \(\theta\) follows a Beta distribution characterized by unknown hyperparameters \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\):

$$\theta \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$$

Analysis with a Fixed Prior Distribution

Before we look at complex hierarchical models, let's start with a simpler approach using a fixed prior. Imagine that by looking at historical data, we find that the tumor probabilities from previous experiments follow a Beta distribution with a known average (mean, \(\mu\)) and a known spread (standard deviation, \(\sigma\)).

It makes sense that tumor probabilities aren't exactly the same in every experiment. This variation happens naturally because of slight differences in the rats themselves, their age, or their environment.

Finding \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) from the Average and Spread

If we know the average (\(\mu\)) and the variance (\(\sigma^2\), which is just the standard deviation squared) of the historical data, we can figure out the exact \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) parameters for our Beta distribution. The formulas for the mean and variance of a \(\text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)\) distribution are:

$$\mu = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta}$$ $$\sigma^2 = \frac{\alpha \beta}{(\alpha + \beta)^2 (\alpha + \beta + 1)} = \frac{\mu(1-\mu)}{\alpha+\beta+1}$$

By doing a little algebra to rearrange these equations, we can find \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) directly:

$$\alpha = \mu \left( \frac{\mu(1-\mu)}{\sigma^2} - 1 \right)$$ $$\beta = (1-\mu) \left( \frac{\mu(1-\mu)}{\sigma^2} - 1 \right)$$

Once we calculate \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) from the historical data, we can use this specific Beta distribution as our fixed prior.

Because we are using a Beta prior with a Binomial model (a combination known as "conjugate"), updating our beliefs with new data is extremely easy. In our current experiment, we observed \(y = 4\) rats with tumors out of \(n = 14\) total rats (meaning 10 rats did not have tumors). To find the new, updated distribution (the posterior) for \(\theta\), we simply add these new counts to our prior parameters:

$$\theta | y \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha + y, \beta + n - y)$$ $$\theta | y \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha + 4, \beta + 10)$$

Approximate Estimate of the Population Distribution using Historical Data

Typically, the true underlying risk of tumors—and its exact mean and standard deviation—is not known in advance. Rather, we only have the raw historical data from past experiments performed on similar groups of rats. In our running example, we have access to historical data from 70 past experiments.

For the \(j\)-th experiment, let \(y_j\) denote the number of rats with tumors, and \(n_j\) denote the total number of rats in that experiment. We assume that these experiments are completely independent of each other. We model each \(y_j\) as independent Binomial data, given the sample size \(n_j\) and the underlying probability of tumor \(\theta_j\):

$$y_j | \theta_j \sim \text{Binomial}(n_j, \theta_j)$$

We assume that the population distribution of these underlying probabilities \(\theta_j\) can be represented by a common Beta distribution, controlled by the hyperparameters \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\):

$$\theta_j | \alpha, \beta \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$$

A Rough Initial Estimate of the Population Parameters

Once this historical data is available, what is the next step? A simple starting point is to directly calculate the raw tumor rates \( y_j / n_j \) for each of the 70 historical experiments, and compute their empirical mean and standard deviation.

# Python Computation

rates = [0/20, 0/20, ..., 9/24]

mean_val = sum(rates) / len(rates)
# Computed Mean: 0.136

std_val = math.sqrt(variance(rates))
# Computed Standard Deviation: 0.103

If we assume that the population distribution of these rates follows a Beta distribution, we can set the mean and standard deviation of our Beta prior to match these empirical values. Using the formulas we derived earlier, we can compute the exact values for the hyperparameters \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\):

Estimated Hyperparameters

\(\alpha \approx 1.36\) | \(\beta \approx 8.62\)

This provides us with a fixed prior of \(\text{Beta}(1.36, 8.62)\).

If we use this simple estimate of the historical population distribution as the prior for our current experiment (\(y=4, n=14\)), the posterior distribution for \(\theta_{71}\) becomes \(\text{Beta}(1.36 + 4, 8.62 + 10)\), which is \(\text{Beta}(5.36, 18.62)\).

Posterior Mean: \(0.224\) Posterior Std. Dev: \(0.083\)

Notice that this posterior mean (\(\approx 0.224\)) is considerably lower than the crude, unpooled estimate from the current experiment alone (\(4/14 \approx 0.286\)). This happens because the weight of the historical experience "pulls" our estimate down, telling us that a tumor proportion as high as \(4/14\) is unusually high compared to the broader population of similar rats.

Assumptions and Variance Inflation

It is important to note that these analyses require a strong assumption: the current tumor risk (\(\theta_{71}\)) and the historical tumor risks (\(\theta_1, \dots, \theta_{70}\)) must be considered a random sample from a common distribution.

This assumption of exchangeability would be invalidated if, for example, it were known that all historical experiments were performed in Laboratory A, but the current data were gathered in Laboratory B. Similarly, if there were significant time trends in the data over the years, treating them all as drawn from the same pool would be inappropriate.

In practice, a simple (although somewhat arbitrary) way to account for potential systematic differences between current and historical data is to artificially inflate the historical variance. For our Beta model, inflating the variance is achieved by decreasing the sum \((\alpha + \beta)\) while holding the ratio \(\alpha / \beta\) (which defines the mean) constant. Other systematic differences, such as explicit time trends in tumor risks, would require a more extensive, structured model.

Wait, is this fully Bayesian?

No. This is not a true Bayesian computation because it is not based on a specific, full probability model. We simply extracted point estimates (the mean and variance) directly from the data and plugged them into the prior formulas.

Having used the 70 historical experiments to form a prior distribution for \(\theta_{71}\), we might now like to use this same prior distribution to obtain Bayesian inferences for the tumor probabilities in the first 70 experiments (\(\theta_1, \dots, \theta_{70}\)). However, there are several logical and practical problems with this approach of directly estimating a prior distribution from existing data:

  • Using Data Twice: If we use the estimated prior distribution for inference about the first 70 experiments, the data is being used twice. First, all the results together are used to estimate the prior distribution, and then each individual experiment's results are used again to estimate its own \(\theta\). This causes us to artificially overestimate our precision.
  • Ignoring Uncertainty: The point estimate for \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) seems arbitrary. By plugging in a single point estimate for these hyperparameters, we necessarily ignore the posterior uncertainty in \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) themselves.
  • Philosophical Consistency: Does it make sense to "estimate" \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) at all? They are part of the "prior" distribution, and according to strict Bayesian logic, priors should be established before the data are gathered.

While this rough approximation serves as an excellent starting point, these limitations highlight the need for a better framework. In the next chapter section, we will resolve these issues by presenting a fully Bayesian approach, treating \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) as unknown parameters with their own hyperprior distributions.

The Logic of Combining Information

Despite the theoretical problems with using point estimates for the prior, it clearly makes more sense to try to estimate the population distribution from all the data—and thereby use it to help estimate each individual \(\theta_j\)—than to estimate all 71 values of \(\theta_j\) completely separately.

Consider the following thought experiment about making inferences on two specific parameters, \(\theta_{26}\) and \(\theta_{27}\). In the historical data, both of these correspond to experiments with identical results: exactly 2 observed tumors out of 20 rats (\(2/20 = 0.10\)).

Suppose our initial population-level prior distribution suggests that the average tumor rate is around \(0.15\). Consequently, our initial posterior estimate for both \(\theta_{26}\) and \(\theta_{27}\) would naturally fall somewhere between the raw data (\(0.10\)) and the prior mean (\(0.15\))—let's say we estimate them to be around \(0.13\).

Now, suppose a divine oracle comes down after you complete your analysis and reveals that the true underlying risk for experiment 26 is exactly \(\theta_{26} = 0.10\). How should this new, absolute knowledge affect your estimate for experiment 27?

If we were analyzing the experiments in total isolation, learning \(\theta_{26}\) would have zero impact on \(\theta_{27}\).

However, because we assume both experiments are drawn from a shared, common population, learning that \(\theta_{26}\) is much lower than our initial prior expectation (\(0.10\) vs \(0.15\)) tells us something profound: it tells us that the overall population of rats actually has a lower baseline tumor risk than we initially thought.

Because our belief about the shared population distribution has shifted downwards, our estimate for \(\theta_{27}\) must logically shift downwards along with it. We would revise our estimate of \(\theta_{27}\) to be lower than our original guess of \(0.13\).

Thus, \(\theta_{26}\) and \(\theta_{27}\) are fundamentally dependent in the posterior distribution, meaning they cannot and should not be analyzed separately. This profound, shared dependency—where learning about one group improves our inferences about all other groups—is the core philosophical and mathematical justification for building hierarchical models.

Conclusion: Empirical vs. Full Hierarchical Bayes

We can retain all the advantages of using the data to estimate prior parameters—while simultaneously eliminating all of the logical disadvantages just mentioned—by putting a probability model on the entire set of parameters and experiments, and then performing a Bayesian analysis on the joint distribution of all the model parameters simultaneously.

The rough approach of using the raw data to plug in point estimates for the prior parameters (which is sometimes called Empirical Bayes) can simply be viewed as a fast, practical approximation to the complete, fully Bayesian hierarchical analysis we will develop next.
