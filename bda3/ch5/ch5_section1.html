Constructing a Parameterized Prior Distribution

Introduction

We begin our exploration of hierarchical models by considering a small-scale experiment. Suppose we have collected data from this current study, alongside data from several similar historical experiments.

Rather than analyzing the new experiment in isolation, we can leverage these historical studies to construct an informed prior distribution. This prior allows us to draw more robust inferences about the parameters of our current experiment.

Mathematically, this approach assumes that the underlying parameters of both the historical and current experiments are random samples drawn from a shared underlying population.

Example. Estimating the risk of tumor in a group of rats

To evaluate a drug for possible medical applications, scientists routinely perform preliminary studies on rodents. Suppose we are analyzing a particular study from the statistical literature where our immediate aim is to find the probability of a tumor, denoted as \(\theta\), in a population of laboratory rats of type 'F344' that receive a zero dose of the drug (i.e., the control group).

The data from the current experiment shows that 4 out of 14 rats developed endometrial stromal polyps.

The Model Setup

Given this data, it is natural to assume a Binomial model for the number of observed tumors \(y\), given the total number of rats \(n\) and the parameter \(\theta\):

$$y | \theta \sim \text{Binomial}(n, \theta)$$

where \(n = 14\) and \(y = 4\).

For mathematical convenience and to achieve conjugacy, we assume that the prior distribution of \(\theta\) follows a Beta distribution characterized by unknown hyperparameters \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\):

$$\theta \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$$

Analysis with a Fixed Prior Distribution

Before we look at complex hierarchical models, let's start with a simpler approach using a fixed prior. Imagine that by looking at historical data, we find that the tumor probabilities from previous experiments follow a Beta distribution with a known average (mean, \(\mu\)) and a known spread (standard deviation, \(\sigma\)).

It makes sense that tumor probabilities aren't exactly the same in every experiment. This variation happens naturally because of slight differences in the rats themselves, their age, or their environment.

Finding \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) from the Average and Spread

If we know the average (\(\mu\)) and the variance (\(\sigma^2\), which is just the standard deviation squared) of the historical data, we can figure out the exact \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) parameters for our Beta distribution. The formulas for the mean and variance of a \(\text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)\) distribution are:

$$\mu = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta}$$ $$\sigma^2 = \frac{\alpha \beta}{(\alpha + \beta)^2 (\alpha + \beta + 1)} = \frac{\mu(1-\mu)}{\alpha+\beta+1}$$

By doing a little algebra to rearrange these equations, we can find \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) directly:

$$\alpha = \mu \left( \frac{\mu(1-\mu)}{\sigma^2} - 1 \right)$$ $$\beta = (1-\mu) \left( \frac{\mu(1-\mu)}{\sigma^2} - 1 \right)$$

Once we calculate \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) from the historical data, we can use this specific Beta distribution as our fixed prior.

Because we are using a Beta prior with a Binomial model (a combination known as "conjugate"), updating our beliefs with new data is extremely easy. In our current experiment, we observed \(y = 4\) rats with tumors out of \(n = 14\) total rats (meaning 10 rats did not have tumors). To find the new, updated distribution (the posterior) for \(\theta\), we simply add these new counts to our prior parameters:

$$\theta | y \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha + y, \beta + n - y)$$ $$\theta | y \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha + 4, \beta + 10)$$

Approximate Estimate of the Population Distribution using Historical Data

Typically, the true underlying risk of tumors—and its exact mean and standard deviation—is not known in advance. Rather, we only have the raw historical data from past experiments performed on similar groups of rats. In our running example, we have access to historical data from 70 past experiments.

For the \(j\)-th experiment, let \(y_j\) denote the number of rats with tumors, and \(n_j\) denote the total number of rats in that experiment. We assume that these experiments are completely independent of each other. We model each \(y_j\) as independent Binomial data, given the sample size \(n_j\) and the underlying probability of tumor \(\theta_j\):

$$y_j | \theta_j \sim \text{Binomial}(n_j, \theta_j)$$

We assume that the population distribution of these underlying probabilities \(\theta_j\) can be represented by a common Beta distribution, controlled by the hyperparameters \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\):

$$\theta_j | \alpha, \beta \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$$
